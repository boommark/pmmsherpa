## What the books establish

### Stanier — the seven questions, repurposed for discovery

Stanier wrote the book for managers, not sellers. But the *discipline* is exactly what discovery needs: say less, ask more, stay curious one beat longer than is comfortable. Six of the seven questions map almost verbatim to discovery stages; one (Foundation — driving beliefs) is too introspective for a buyer call and is dropped:

| Stanier question | Discovery role |
|---|---|
| Kickstart — "What's on your mind?" | Opener after the frame is set |
| AWE — "And what else?" | The probe. Used after every substantive answer. The single highest-leverage question in the book |
| Focus — "What's the real challenge here for you?" | Pain root-cause; cuts past the presenting symptom |
| Foundation (driving beliefs) | DROPPED — too introspective for a buyer call |
| Lazy — "What do you want?" | Aspiration anchor |
| Strategic — "What would need to be true for that to work?" | Consensus / kill-criteria question (operationalizes MEDDIC's "decision criteria" without interrogating) |
| Learning — "What was most useful here for you?" | Closes the call, anchors recall, surfaces what landed |

Stanier's discipline rules become the system prompt:
- **Ask one question at a time** — don't rapid-fire
- **Cut the intro** — long preambles dilute the question
- **Stay curious** — fake curiosity is transparent
- **Listen to the answer** — don't prepare your next thought while they talk
- **No rhetorical questions** — "Have you thought of X?" when you mean "you should do X" is feature-pitching, not discovery

### Voss — calibrated-question phrasing

Voss's contribution is structural: **calibrated questions** ("how" and "what", never "why") give the prospect the illusion of control while letting the seller steer. Specifics adopted:

- Open with **"how"** or **"what"**. "Why" puts people on defense ("why are you using spreadsheets?" reads as a judgment).
- **Mirroring** — repeat the last 3 words as a question, let silence do the work. Surfaces the layer beneath the polite answer.
- **Labeling emotions** — "It seems like…", "It sounds like…" — disarms defensiveness, surfaces what's really going on.
- **Get to "No"** — premature "yes" is the most dangerous answer in B2B sales. Frame questions so it's safe for the prospect to say no ("Is now a bad time?" outperforms "Do you have a few minutes?").
- **Black Swan** — there is one piece of information the seller doesn't know they don't know. Open-ended probing surfaces it.

### Weinberg — pain qualification + the failure-mode list

Weinberg is the discipline source for *what* to qualify on (pain, sponsor, urgency, fit) and a sharp catalog of *how reps fail*. The single most-quoted Weinberg failure mode is the show-up-and-throw-up — spewing features and benefits the moment the rep gets a chance to talk. Discovery questions that are pitches with question marks are this failure mode in question form. Other Not-So-Sweet-16 items adopted:

- Not qualifying early enough (need urgency + sponsor on the table by question 5–6)
- Letting the prospect control the process (the call has an agenda and the rep runs it)
- Pursuing wrong-fit opportunities (early disqualification is a feature, not a bug)
- Weak follow-through (the close question — "what was most useful for you?" — sets up the next step)

Weinberg's Power Statement (problems-we-solve → offering → proof → CTA) is **not a discovery framework** — it's the *frame the rep delivers BEFORE discovery* so the prospect knows why to engage. The template treats Power-Statement-style framing as Step 0 (the frame), then opens discovery.

### Where the books overlap and merge cleanly

- Stanier's "stay curious" + Weinberg's "no show-up-and-throw-up" + Voss's "let them talk" all converge on the same rule: **the rep should be talking less than 30% of the time on a discovery call.**
- Stanier's "ask one question at a time" + Voss's "calibrated phrasing" + Weinberg's "letting prospect control = failure" all support the same structural choice: **the question set is a *menu*, not a script.** The rep pulls 8–12 questions from the menu in conversation order, not all 30+.

### Where they conflict — and which wins

- **Voss vs. Weinberg on qualification phrasing.** Weinberg's discipline pushes for direct B2B qualification (sponsor, urgency, fit). Voss says interrogating destroys the call. **Resolution: Weinberg wins on *what* to qualify (the categories); Voss wins on *how* to phrase (calibrated, not BANT-style interrogation).** This is the central design decision: the qualification section uses MEDDIC-style categories but Voss-style phrasing.

- **Stanier's "What do you want?" (Lazy Question) vs. B2B subtlety.** Stanier's literal phrasing reads blunt in a managerial context but slightly off in a B2B sales call where the prospect may not have decided what they want. We soften to "If this was working the way you wanted six months from now, what would be different?" — same intent, more aspirational frame. Stanier wins on intent; corpus practitioner phrasing wins on the literal sentence.

- **MEDDIC vs. PACTT vs. BANT.** Corpus references all three. Books don't pick a winner. **Resolution: structure the qualification section around what each framework agrees on (metrics, economic buyer, decision criteria, decision process, identified pain, urgency-trigger) and avoid acronym lock-in.** The template doesn't say "MEDDIC" — it says "what does success look like in numbers?" and "who has to sign off?" — operationalizing the underlying questions without religion.

## Corpus research (amplification)

The corpus query returned 10 citations. Top ones that informed the template:

1. **Mike Weinberg — New Sales Simplified, p.141 + p.144** — Direct echo of Power Statement and the qualification discipline. Reinforced the book card.
2. **Joel Klettke — Punchy, p.36–37** — Punchy's voice-of-customer interviewing rules (open questions, probe, what's-broken-today). Supports Stanier's discipline from a copywriter angle. Adopted as failure-mode language.
3. **Steli Efti — Product Demos that Sell, p.15** — Discovery-before-demo pattern. Adopted as system-prompt rule: discovery is *not* a demo prelude; demoing inside discovery is a failure mode.
4. **Geoffrey A. Moore — Crossing the Chasm, p.154–155** — Pragmatist buyer's evaluation criteria (whole product, references, vendor stability). Informs the *competitive context* category — what bar are they evaluating against, where did the bar come from.
5. **Maja Voje — The Go To Market Strategist, p.291** — GTM discovery patterns; reinforces structure but adds nothing net-new.
6. **Gabriel Weinberg & Justin Mares — Traction, p.133** — Traction's customer interviewing patterns. Aligns with Stanier discipline.
7. **Matt Heng — PMA "Five actionable strategies for collaborating with sales teams"** — Discovery section practitioner POV. Adopted: the doctor's-intake frame ("I work with X who hit Y wall — is that you, or is it different?") as the opener pattern.

**Net-new from corpus (not in books):**
- The **doctor's-intake frame** as the opener pattern — surfaces credibility, orients the prospect, makes them want to be examined.
- The **"who would notice?" aspiration question** — deals die when the buyer can't explain value to someone else. Tests internal consensus more sharply than "who else is involved?"
- The **"where did that bar come from?" competitive question** — surfaces who actually owns the decision criteria (often a board member or competitor benchmark, not the buyer).
- The **"what would kill this deal?" question** — get to "No" (Voss) operationalized for B2B. Most reps fear this question; it's the one that tells you whether you're in a real deal.

**Corpus gaps:** Stanier himself did not surface in the citation list. The book is in the corpus but the Coaching Habit's discovery applicability isn't well-tagged. Logged in `corpus-gaps.md`. Not blocking — the book card is canonical and we have it in hand.

## Template design decisions

**Authority hierarchy:** Stanier provides the *discipline* (one question at a time, AWE probe, no rhetorical questions). Voss provides the *phrasing* (calibrated, mirror, label, get to No). Weinberg provides the *qualification spine* (pain, sponsor, urgency, fit). Corpus amplifies with B2B-specific phrasings.

**Structure: a menu of categories, not a script.** Eight categories, each with 3–6 calibrated questions. The rep pulls in conversation order — they do NOT read all 30+ questions sequentially. This is the central distinction from a *talk track* (artifact 21), which sequences a pitch. The discovery question set is a *bank* the rep draws from.

**Eight categories, fixed order:**

1. **Frame & Setup** — the doctor's-intake opener BEFORE any question. Power Statement (Weinberg) condensed to one paragraph. This is what the rep says, not asks.
2. **Opener Questions** — Stanier's Kickstart adapted. 2–3 questions.
3. **Pain — current state & what's broken** — Stanier's Focus + Weinberg's pain qualification. 5–6 questions including "what have you tried?" (the single highest-signal question per corpus).
4. **Aspiration — what good looks like** — Stanier's Lazy adapted. 3–4 questions including the "who would notice?" consensus probe.
5. **Buying Process — sponsor, consensus, kill criteria** — Stanier's Strategic + Voss's get-to-No. 4–5 questions including "what would kill this?".
6. **Competitive Context** — corpus-driven. 3–4 questions; never "who else are you looking at?" — instead the bar-and-origin question.
7. **Qualification — metrics, urgency, fit** — Weinberg discipline + Voss phrasing. MEDDIC content, calibrated phrasing. 4–5 questions.
8. **Close — Stanier's Learning Question + advance commitment.** 2 questions.

**Calibrated prefixes only.** Every question template uses "how" / "what" / "walk me through" / "tell me about". "Why" is explicitly forbidden in the system prompt (Voss).

**AWE probe baked into every section.** Each category ends with a "and what else?" probe note — reminding the rep that the second answer is usually richer than the first.

**Mirroring + labeling cues, not full questions.** Mirroring and labeling aren't standalone questions — they're conversational moves. The template includes a *Conversational Moves* sidebar with 4 mirror/label patterns to use throughout.

**Disqualification is a feature.** A small *Disqualifying Signals* block tells the rep what answers should kill the deal early — before another month of pursuing a no-pain prospect.

**Inheritance:** the template inherits buyer persona (05) and ICP (04) for *who* to qualify and what pains to probe on, but does NOT duplicate persona content. If those artifacts exist, the rep reads them first; if not, the template prompts the rep to draft a thumbnail persona before generating questions.

## System prompt failure modes (negative guidance)

Distilled to 8 (consistent with messaging framework template's negative-heavy ratio for high-craft documents):

1. **Show-up-and-throw-up (Weinberg)** — feature-pitching disguised as questions. "Have you considered that our automation engine could…" is a pitch with a question mark, not discovery.
2. **Leading questions** — "You'd agree manual processes are a problem, right?" Doesn't learn anything. Just gets agreement that creates false confidence.
3. **"Why" as opener** — "why are you doing X?" reads as judgment. Voss is unambiguous: "how" and "what" only.
4. **BANT-style interrogation** — Budget/Authority/Need/Timeline run as a checklist makes the prospect feel processed, not heard.
5. **Rapid-fire questioning (Stanier)** — multiple questions stacked in one breath. Ask one, listen fully, AWE-probe, then move on.
6. **Skipping the AWE probe** — the second answer is usually richer than the first. Reps who don't probe collect surface answers and lose the deal.
7. **No frame, no Power Statement** — diving into questions cold. The prospect hasn't been told why this conversation matters or what the rep can help with.
8. **No disqualification willingness** — pursuing a prospect who has tried nothing and feels no pain. If the answers don't qualify, the call ends — that's a feature, not a failure.

## Positive asks

1. **Calibrated phrasing only** — every question begins with "how", "what", "walk me through", or "tell me about".
2. **One question at a time** — the menu is for the rep; the conversation is one question, listen, probe, advance.
3. **Inherit ICP and Buyer Persona** — if those artifacts exist, customize pain and qualification questions to that persona; otherwise prompt the user for a thumbnail persona first.
4. **Mirror and label between questions** — the conversational moves sidebar is not optional decoration; it's how the rep stays curious one beat longer.

## Open questions for audit

- **Should the Conversational Moves sidebar be a separate section or embedded in each category?** Currently a sidebar so it stays visible. Argument for embedding: forces the rep to use it per-category. Argument for sidebar (chosen): doesn't bloat each category.
- **Should we render 30+ questions or 20-ish?** Currently 30–35 across 8 categories. Argument for fewer: reps won't read 35. Argument for keeping (chosen): it's a *menu* — reps pick 8–12 per call. Quantity gives them coverage of edge cases (price-sensitive deal, competitive deal, executive call).
- **Should "what would kill this deal?" be in Buying Process or Qualification?** Currently Buying Process. It's borderline — could go either place. Choosing Buying Process because it's about *consensus and blockers*, not whether the buyer is qualified.
- **MEDDIC names in the qualification section — yes or no?** Currently *no*. The template uses the underlying questions without naming the framework. This keeps it framework-agnostic for users on PACTT, MEDDPICC, or custom criteria. Open for revision if user audit prefers explicit naming.
