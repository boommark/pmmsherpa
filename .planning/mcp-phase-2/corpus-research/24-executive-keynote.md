## What the books establish (combined)

### Resonate — the architecture
- **Sparkline.** The What Is / What Could Be oscillation is the structural spine of any presentation that has to *move* an audience (not inform). For a keynote, the oscillation is deeper than for a launch deck — the keynote's job is to seed a *belief shift*, not announce a product.
- **Audience-as-Hero / Mentor Stance.** The exec is the mentor (Yoda), not the hero (Luke). For executive keynotes this is the single hardest discipline: every CEO's instinct is to use the keynote stage to narrate the *company's* journey. Resonate is unambiguous: the audience is the hero; the company is the guide.
- **S.T.A.R. Moment.** One planted dramatic beat — the "no software" logo, the pocket-iPod reveal, a single shocking number. For a keynote (vs. launch deck) the S.T.A.R. moment is more often a *symbolic* artifact than a customer story — it has to encapsulate the Big Idea, not the product.
- **Frequency Tuning.** Know the audience's existing beliefs before drafting. For a keynote the audience varies wildly by venue (customer event = validation-seekers; conference = peers + skeptics; all-hands = employees needing direction). The *audience read* drives every other choice.
- **Big Idea.** One sentence. Memorable. Plain language. The thing the audience repeats the next morning.

### Illuminate — the torchbearer/change-comm layer
- **Torchbearer Model.** A leader uses *speeches, stories, ceremonies, and symbols* to illuminate a path through change. Executive keynotes are the speech node of this toolkit; symbols (logos, props, on-stage demos as ceremonies) extend the keynote beyond the slide deck.
- **Venture Scape.** Epic narrative spanning past, present, future. For a category-creation keynote, the Venture Scape *is* the spine — interpret what changed, where the world is going, why this room matters.
- **Threshold Crossing.** The keynote is the *threshold moment* — the audience has to choose to commit to the new world. The CTA is the threshold ask, not a marketing action.
- **Empathetic Listening (pre-work).** Before drafting, the torchbearer must understand what travelers (audience) fear, hope for, resist. This is where most exec keynotes fail — they are written from the exec's POV, not the audience's.

### TED — the delivery layer
- **Idea Worth Spreading.** Lock the single idea before slides exist. For an exec keynote, this is the Big Idea — and the locking sentence is non-negotiable pre-work.
- **Opening Hook (first 30 seconds).** Not "thanks for being here." A provocation: question, revelation, unexpected statement. Donovan is firm — first sentence, no warm-up.
- **Story-Evidence Loop.** Alternate narrative and proof. For a keynote this beat is heavier on narrative than a sales pitch; data exists to ground the story, not the other way around.
- **Closing Circle.** End where you opened, reframed by everything in between. The CTA *answers* the opening hook.
- **Time Discipline.** TED's 18-minute ceiling is the lower bound for executive keynotes. Industry norm: 18-30 min. Conference keynotes can stretch to 40-45 min but the corpus and books agree clarity collapses beyond 30 unless there is a planned interactive or panel break.
- **Slide Strategy.** Slides support, never replace. Images over text. One idea per slide. Never read the slide aloud.
- **Rehearsal protocol.** Donovan's distinctive contribution. The talk must work *without* slides. If the written prose script doesn't hold together standalone, the structure isn't there. Rehearsal is structural validation, not just polish.

## Corpus research (amplification only)

Top citations from query "structure of an executive keynote, vision/category-creation talk, Big Idea, story spine, S.T.A.R. moment, single CTA, failure modes":

- **Duarte — Resonate** (pp. 15, 17, 113, 114) — book card already covered these
- **Ramadan/Peterson/Lochhead/Maney — Play Bigger** (pp. 86, 87, 88, 118) — *category-creation* lens. New contribution: the keynote isn't a product announcement, it's a *category claim*. The Big Idea names a shift the audience now has to navigate. "We call this X" — the category exists whether your company does or not; you're the one who saw it first.
- **Marty Cagan — Empowered** (pp. 82, 194) — adjacent. Surfaced because the query mentioned vision; Cagan's vision-narrative discipline echoes the prose-first / write-without-slides rule. Net contribution: rehearsal as *structural validation*, not delivery polish.

### What corpus added beyond the books
1. **Category-creation framing.** Play Bigger sharpens the "Big Idea" into a *category claim* — name the new way of thinking ("we call this X"). The category exists independent of the company. This is sharper than Resonate's generic "Big Idea" for category-creation contexts. **Adopted as the Big Idea framing in pre-work.**
2. **Story spine ordering with explicit minute budgets.** Corpus rendered a 7-beat spine (World Has Changed → Old Way Broken → New Category → Audience as Hero → S.T.A.R. → Vision → CTA) with specific minute budgets per beat. Adopted as the script skeleton.
3. **The exec-as-hero failure mode named explicitly.** Books cover audience-as-hero positively; corpus phrases the trap negatively ("We built this. We saw the future. We solved it.") which is sharper for system-prompt suppression.
4. **Prose-first rehearsal rule.** "Write the full talk in prose first, iterate until it's logical and emotionally coherent, then build slides from it." This collapses Donovan's rehearsal protocol and Cagan's vision-narrative discipline into one operational rule. Adopted as a pre-work constraint AND a validation gate.
5. **One-line opener test.** "Open with the market shift. First sentence, no warm-up. The audience decides in sixty seconds whether this is worth their attention." Sharper than TED's "first 30 seconds" framing for keynote contexts.

### Where books and corpus disagreed
No direct conflicts. Corpus reads as a tight synthesis of Resonate + Play Bigger + TED, with sharper phrasing for category-creation contexts. Where books are sharper conceptually (mentor stance, torchbearer model, threshold crossing), books win. Where corpus is sharper operationally (the 7-beat spine with minutes, prose-first rehearsal, explicit failure modes), corpus wins.

### Where they merged
- **Sparkline + Venture Scape + Story Spine** — three names for one shape. Resonate's oscillation is the rhythm; Illuminate's Venture Scape is the time horizon; the corpus 7-beat spine is the operational sequence. Merged into a single 7-beat script skeleton with each beat tagged What Is / What Could Be.
- **Threshold Crossing + Single CTA** — Illuminate's threshold and TED's closing-circle CTA collapse into one slot. The CTA is the audience's commit moment.
- **S.T.A.R. moment + symbolic artifact** — Resonate's S.T.A.R. taxonomy (stat/story/quote/image/symbol) plus Illuminate's symbolic-artifacts framework. For executive keynotes, *symbol* and *image* dominate (Salesforce "no software" logo, Apple pocket iPod) more than they do for launch decks (where customer-story is the more common S.T.A.R.).

## Template design decisions

**This artifact is dual-format: script + deck outline.** Unlike artifact 14 (launch deck = deck spec only) and artifact 13 (internal launch deck = enablement deck), an executive keynote is delivered live by a CEO/CMO — the script is the primary artifact, the deck is the visual support. The skeleton renders both: a 7-beat verbal script *and* a slide-by-slide deck outline keyed to the script beats.

**Spine = corpus 7-beat story spine, validated against Sparkline.** The corpus spine is operationally sharper than rendering Sparkline beats abstractly. Each beat is tagged What Is / What Could Be so the Sparkline oscillation is enforced inside the spine.

**Big Idea = category claim, locked in pre-work.** Adopting Play Bigger's category-creation framing because executive keynotes (vs. launch decks) are most often category/vision talks. If the user is delivering a non-category-creation keynote (e.g., culture all-hands), the same Big Idea pre-work still applies — they just substitute "the shift this room is navigating" for "the new category."

**Audience read in pre-work.** Three named venues — customer event, conference, all-hands — each with a different audience-as-hero read. Frequency Tuning forced into pre-work because exec keynotes deliver to wildly different audiences and the script changes register accordingly.

**Prose-first rule.** Adopted from corpus (and reinforced by TED + Cagan). The skeleton renders the script first, deck outline second. The script must hold together as prose without the deck. Validation gate at the end re-asserts this.

**Time budget tiered.** 18-22 min (TED-shape, conference talk), 25-30 min (typical exec keynote, customer event), 35-45 min (extended conference keynote, big tent event). Pre-work locks the budget; skeleton sizes accordingly.

**Single CTA = threshold ask.** From Illuminate. The CTA is the audience's commit moment, not a marketing action. "Join the [movement/category/initiative]" is a threshold ask; "visit our booth" is a shrug.

**Rehearsal protocol as final block.** Donovan's distinctive contribution. Three rehearsal gates: prose-only read-through (does the script hold without slides?), timed read-through (does it fit the budget?), audience-as-hero check (read every paragraph — is the subject the audience or the company?).

**Boundary against artifact 14 (launch deck).** Launch deck answers "why now (about this product)?". Keynote answers "why this shift?". Launch deck is product-anchored; keynote is vision/category-anchored. The keynote can announce a product, but the product is not the spine — the shift is.

**Boundary against artifact 13 (internal launch deck).** Internal launch is employee enablement for a specific launch (RACI, talk track, Q&A appendix). Executive keynote is broader — it is delivered live by an exec to any audience.

**Boundary against artifact 36 (webinar deck).** Webinar is shorter (often 30-45 min including Q&A), interactive, instructional. Keynote is performance — single voice, vision-led, no audience interaction in the keynote frame itself.

## System prompt failure modes (negative guidance)

From book + corpus, distilled to 9 — keynote-specific:

1. **Feature-pitching disguised as keynote** — every "vision" slide drifts back to capabilities. Fix: prose-first rule.
2. **Exec-as-hero** — "we built this, we saw the future, we solved it." The audience stops listening.
3. **Multiple Big Ideas** — three category claims means zero land. One talk, one idea.
4. **Weak opening** — "thanks for being here, I'm excited to share..." is thirty seconds of lost trust.
5. **No planted S.T.A.R. moment** — equally-weighted beats produce no memorable moment.
6. **Multiple CTAs** — "visit our booth, download our report, talk to your AE, sign up for beta." One threshold ask.
7. **Hero's Journey drift** — same as artifacts 03 and 14. B2B audiences don't take fantasy quests.
8. **Vendor name-drops as enemies** — the enemy is the OLD WAY, not a named competitor. Combat register collapses inspiration.
9. **No rehearsal / slide-first drafting** — the talk must work without slides. If prose doesn't hold, the structure isn't there.

## Open questions for audit

- **Should script and deck outline be rendered as one interleaved document, or two clearly separated sections?** Currently rendered as: full script first (7 beats), then a slide-per-beat deck outline keyed to the script. Alternative: per-beat blocks where each beat shows script + slide together. Chose separated because (a) prose-first rule is structurally enforced by writing the script before the deck, (b) the script is the primary artifact and a CEO will edit prose without wanting deck noise.
- **Should the Big Idea pre-work be category-creation-specific or kept generic?** Currently generic with a category-creation example. Argument for forcing category framing: keynotes that aren't category claims often shouldn't be exec keynotes (could be a webinar or launch deck). Argument against: too prescriptive — culture all-hands and inspirational keynotes are valid keynote frames that aren't category claims.
- **Should the threshold ask be one CTA or zero CTA in some venues?** All-hands keynotes sometimes have no external CTA — the "ask" is a commitment internal to the company. Currently the skeleton calls for a single threshold ask but allows the user to interpret it broadly (commit-action, not necessarily a marketing action).
- **Corpus gap:** Donovan's *How to Deliver a TED Talk* did not surface in the corpus query (no TED book chunks in the citations). The book card was the source. Logged in `corpus-gaps.md` (no new entry needed if already there for presentations cluster).
