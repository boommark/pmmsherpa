## What the books establish (combined)

### Resonate — the architecture
- **Sparkline.** The What Is / What Could Be oscillation drives the QBR's emotional spine: where the customer started (What Is — the problem they signed to solve) → what got delivered (What Could Be, made real) → where they are today (What Is, current state) → where customers at their stage typically go next (What Could Be, extended). Without this oscillation, the deck flattens into a dashboard.
- **Audience-as-Hero / Mentor Stance.** The single hardest discipline in QBRs: every CS / AM team's instinct is to use the QBR to recap *what we did*. The customer is the hero of the year. The vendor is the mentor (Yoda) showing them the path forward. Every body slide must have the customer's outcome — not the vendor's activity — as the subject of the headline.
- **S.T.A.R. Moment.** One planted dramatic beat — most often "the one number they haven't seen before" or the inflection-point story on the adoption curve. The QBR's S.T.A.R. moment earns the renewal: it is the line the executive sponsor repeats in their next leadership meeting.
- **Frequency Tuning.** QBR audiences split: account-team QBR (single named customer, exec sponsor in the room) vs. customer all-hands (broader event, hundreds of customers, mixed seniority). The audience read drives the variant. Same Sparkline; different register.

### Smart Brevity — the compression layer
- **Core 4 per slide.** Each slide is a Smart Brevity unit: TEASE (the slide title), LEDE (the headline number / outcome), WHY IT MATTERS (the business translation), GO DEEPER (one supporting beat — quote, anecdote, sub-metric). Three bullet points without why-it-matters fails Smart Brevity.
- **Audience-First.** "Strip every word that doesn't serve the reader." The exec sponsor is reading cold. Vendor jargon, internal product names, feature-list language all fail. Translate every metric into the buyer's business language ("case resolution down 32% = 6 hours per week back to your team").
- **Short-Not-Shallow.** Three metrics, three quotes, one ask. Depth comes from precision, not volume. The instinct to "show all the work" is the failure mode — pick the three numbers that connect to *their* stated business goals.

### Corpus — operational patterns
- **Outcomes-not-activities.** "Reduced manual processing time by 40%" lands. "Completed 14 onboarding sessions" doesn't. The corpus is unambiguous: the buyer does not care about vendor effort; they care about their own business outcome.
- **Three-quote champion pattern.** One day-to-day user (emotional, specific) + one manager (efficiency, team impact) + one stakeholder near the exec sponsor (strategic framing). Together they answer the question the exec sponsor is quietly asking: *"Was this the right call?"*
- **Expansion-as-natural-progression.** Frame expansion as "where customers at your stage typically go next, and why," not as a roadmap pitch. Maturity model + their position on it + what unlocks at the next stage in *their* outcomes language. Expansion dressed as renewal collapses trust; expansion as trusted-adviser framing earns the next conversation.
- **Exec sponsor as cold reader.** The exec sponsor was often not in the room when the deal was signed. The deck must give them the origin story (slide 2: villain / pain) so the transformation lands harder.
- **Commitments-vs-actuals scorecard.** Implicit in the corpus's "Your Team's Journey" + "Our Commitment" pattern. The QBR earns trust by showing what was committed last quarter and what was delivered against it — before asking for the next commit.
- **The retention story and the expansion story are the same story.** "Customers who feel seen expand. The deck that proves value is the deck that earns the next conversation." Adopted as the central design rule.

## Branching: QBR vs. all-hands

This artifact has two variants chosen in pre-work:

- **Account-team QBR** — single named customer, ~6-12 attendees, exec sponsor in the room. Goal: prove value, scorecard commitments, secure renewal, surface expansion. Default time budget 30-45 min. The three-quote pattern uses the customer's *own* champions; the roadmap is keyed to *their* stated priorities; the Ask is renewal/expansion-specific.
- **Customer all-hands** — broader customer event keynote (Customer Day, user conference, virtual all-hands), 100s-1000s of customers, mixed seniority. Goal: prove aggregate value across the customer base, align on shared roadmap, drive community. Default time budget 25-35 min. The three-quote pattern uses *representative* customers (one SMB, one mid-market, one enterprise — or by industry); the roadmap is the platform-level direction; the Ask is participation (beta enrollment, advisory board, breakouts).

The skeleton is the same 8 slides; pre-work selects the variant and the bracketed prompts adapt. **One template, branching pre-work** — chosen over two separate templates because the Sparkline spine, audience-as-hero stance, and Smart Brevity compression are identical. Diverging only in pre-work and bracket guidance keeps the maintenance surface low.

## Template design decisions

**Spine = corpus 8-slide skeleton, validated against Sparkline.** Each slide is tagged for its What-Is / What-Could-Be position so the oscillation is enforced inside the spine.

**Audience-as-hero is the single hardest discipline.** Negative system prompt explicitly bans "look what we built" headlines. Validation gate at the end re-asserts: read every body slide; is the subject the customer's outcome or the vendor's activity?

**Smart Brevity per slide.** Each slide skeleton enforces TEASE (title) → LEDE (headline outcome) → WHY IT MATTERS (business translation) → GO DEEPER (one supporting beat). Failure mode: three bullets without why-it-matters.

**Three-quote pattern is structural, not optional.** The role-mapping (user / manager / stakeholder) is in the bracket guidance for slide 5. For all-hands variant, becomes (SMB / mid-market / enterprise) or (industry-A / industry-B / industry-C).

**Commitments-vs-actuals scorecard.** Slide 4 ("Your Team's Journey") plus an explicit pre-work prompt for the prior-quarter commitments. Negative system prompt bans skipping this — without commitments-vs-actuals, the QBR is a feature recap.

**Expansion as trusted-adviser, not pitch.** Slide 6 is framed as maturity model + their position + what-unlocks-next, in *their* outcomes language. Negative system prompt bans "expansion pitch dressed as renewal" — the corpus's sharpest failure mode.

**Single Ask.** Slide 8. Smart Brevity discipline. Multiple asks = zero asks.

**Boundary against siblings.**
- vs. artifact 14 (customer_launch_deck): launch deck is broadcast, marketing-led, prospect+customer dual audience, ends in event-CTA. QBR is delivered TO an existing customer's stakeholders, value-led, customer is the hero of the recap, ends in renewal/expansion ask.
- vs. artifact 24 (executive_keynote): keynote answers "why this shift?" with the company as mentor on a vision arc. QBR answers "did the bet pay off and what's next for *you*?" — the customer's own arc, not a market shift.
- vs. artifact 19 (sales_pitch_deck): pitch deck is pre-purchase, answers "why us?". QBR is post-purchase, answers "was the bet right and where do we go from here?".

## Open questions for user audit

1. **Variant default.** Skeleton defaults to account-team QBR (the more common case). All-hands variant is selectable in pre-work. Acceptable, or should there be two separate templates?
2. **Time budgets.** Defaulting to 30-45 min (account QBR) and 25-35 min (all-hands). Confirm these match Abhishek's customer-success/PMM practitioner expectation.
3. **Commitments-vs-actuals depth.** Currently a single slide (slide 4 + scorecard sub-section). For mature CS organizations this could warrant its own slide. Flagging for post-launch revision if usage shows the scorecard is consistently under-rendered.
4. **Expansion slide language.** Used "Roadmap Ahead" per corpus. PMM Sherpa users might prefer "What's Next" (less salesy). Bracket guidance allows both.
