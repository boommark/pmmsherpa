# Research — Internal Launch FAQ

## Canonical sources (read FIRST)

- **Smart Brevity** (VandeHei / Allen / Schwartz) — primary craft source
  - Card: `~/Documents/AbhishekR/Book Brain/Smart Brevity.md`
  - Core 4 structure: TEASE → LEDE → WHY IT MATTERS → GO DEEPER. The Q+A pair
    is the unit of an FAQ; Smart Brevity gives us the per-pair discipline.
  - "Audience First Principle" — every word must serve the reader (here:
    the AE / SE / CSM / support rep / exec). Not the PMM author.
  - "Short, Not Shallow" — concise ≠ incomplete. The internal FAQ is the
    place this principle is *most* abused (PMMs fear short answers will look
    underbaked, so they pad them).
  - "Fog of Words" — the failure mode for an internal FAQ specifically:
    marketing copy pasted in as answers. Reps need plain-language operational
    answers, not press-release prose.

## What Smart Brevity establishes for this artifact

A useful FAQ is **a series of TEASE/LEDE pairs**, not a wall of paragraphs:

- **Question = TEASE.** It's the hook the reader actually has. If the question
  isn't one a real rep would ask, the answer is irrelevant. (Failure mode:
  "What is the strategic vision behind this product?" — nobody asks that.
  They ask "what is it.")
- **Answer's first sentence = LEDE.** The decision-grade insight. A rep
  scanning the doc on a Slack call should be able to read just the first
  sentence and have a usable answer.
- **Optional GO DEEPER.** Context, edge cases, links to deck/battlecard. Off
  by default — only added when the question genuinely has nuance.
- **WHY IT MATTERS** is collapsed for internal FAQs: every Q is by definition
  one a rep would ask, so the stakes are implicit. (We don't need a "why
  this question matters" preamble — that's marketing-doc bloat.)

Smart Brevity's principles applied to an FAQ also prescribe:
- One idea per answer. If two come up, split into two questions.
- Plain language. If a CSM has to translate the FAQ for the customer, the
  FAQ is already failing.
- Length cap per answer. Aim 2-4 sentences for the LEDE, optional 3-5 bullets
  for GO DEEPER. Anything longer is a different artifact (one-pager, deck).

## Boundary calls (what this is NOT)

- **NOT the partner-facing FAQ (artifact 30).** This is for *employees* —
  AEs, SEs, CSMs, support, execs. Different sensitivity: we can name
  competitors, name known issues, name pricing exceptions, name what's on
  the roadmap. The partner FAQ has to assume an external reader.
- **NOT the customer-facing FAQ.** Customer FAQs answer evaluation questions
  ("Is my data secure?"). Internal FAQs answer operational questions ("What
  do I say when a customer asks if their data is secure?").
- **NOT a battlecard.** The battlecard (artifact 16) is the deep competitive
  doc. The internal FAQ has a *competitive section*, but it points to the
  battlecard for depth.
- **NOT the launch deck.** The deck (artifact 13) is the narrative. The FAQ
  is reference material reps consult between meetings.

## Inherited inputs

- **Positioning (01)** — drives the "what is it" answer and the "how it
  competes" framing.
- **Competitive (16, battlecard)** — the FAQ summarizes; the battlecard is
  the source of truth.
- **Pricing (39)** — the FAQ answers the operational pricing questions
  reps get; the pricing page copy is the customer-facing artifact.

The FAQ is intentionally *downstream* of these. If a launch ships without
positioning + battlecard locked, the FAQ will paper over the gap with
marketing prose — which is the #1 failure mode the corpus surfaced.

### What the corpus added beyond Smart Brevity (net-new)

Smart Brevity gives the *craft discipline* (TEASE/LEDE per pair). The corpus
gave the **category architecture** — which questions actually belong, drawn
from real practitioner experience launching B2B SaaS:

1. **"What does it NOT do yet?"** — The single most-skipped question in
   internal FAQs and the most-asked question in the field. Reps will invent
   an answer if you don't supply one.
2. **"Who is NOT a good fit?"** — More valuable for CSMs handling expansion
   calls than the ICP positive case. Skipped in most launch FAQs.
3. **"Where do competitors have a legitimate advantage?"** — Reps will find
   out anyway. Better they hear it from PMM than from a lost deal.
4. **"What's the do-nothing case?"** — In B2B SaaS, the most common
   competitor is the status quo. Most FAQs miss it because they only address
   *named* competitors.
5. **Scripted objection responses, not principles** — The corpus is emphatic:
   "Script a response, not a principle." Reps need actual words.
6. **Known issues section** — The two or three things that aren't perfect
   yet. Withholding these turns reps who get blindsided into the loudest
   internal critics.
7. **SE handoff trigger** — Specific trigger ("when X comes up"), not "when
   it gets technical."
8. **Discount authority + exception path** — Vague pricing answers destroy
   credibility with AEs faster than anything else.
9. **Roadmap-as-shipped warning** — Explicit "don't say this" for forward-
   looking statements that aren't safe.
10. **Existing-account filter, not philosophy** — "Give them a filter, not
    a philosophy" for which accounts to call first.

### Where Smart Brevity and the corpus aligned

- Plain-language answers (BBQ-Talk equivalent): both sources insist.
- Brevity per answer: Smart Brevity prescribes; corpus reinforces ("at 2pm
  on a Thursday a rep is scanning, not reading").
- Audience-first: Smart Brevity's principle; corpus operationalises it as
  "write this like you're explaining it to a smart AE who just got back
  from vacation."

### Where they merged

- **Q+A unit structure.** Smart Brevity's TEASE/LEDE/(GO DEEPER) becomes the
  required shape of every Q+A. Each answer must lead with a single decision-
  grade sentence, with optional bullets for nuance.
- **"Don't say this" section.** Corpus pattern (talking points + what to
  avoid). Smart Brevity's "Fog of Words" critique sharpens this — bad
  internal FAQs let reps say things that *are* fog (overclaims, jargon,
  roadmap-as-shipped). The "don't say this" block is the legal/credibility
  guardrail. Phrased per Smart Brevity: short, blunt, scannable.

## Template design decisions

**Craft discipline = Smart Brevity TEASE/LEDE/(GO DEEPER) per Q+A.** This is
the single most important structural choice. Without it, the model produces
paragraph-shaped marketing answers that fail the "rep at 2pm Thursday" test.
The skeleton enforces this by giving every answer block two required slots
(LEDE first, optional GO DEEPER bullets) and a hard length cap.

**Category architecture = corpus synthesis.** Eight required sections plus
one "objection handling" addendum, drawn directly from the corpus output and
Sharebird/PMA practice:

1. What is it
2. Who's it for
3. Why now
4. Pricing
5. How it competes
6. Sales process
7. Support model
8. Talking points + what to avoid (combined — the contrast is what makes
   both useful)
9. Objection handling (standalone — scripted responses, not principles)

A 10th block — **Known issues** — is included as a required closer per the
corpus's "one more thing" guidance. Most PMMs are afraid to write it; that's
exactly why it shipping with reps' eyes on it pre-launch is a value-add.

**Sections render as Q+A pairs, not narrative.** Each section opens with a
one-line section purpose, then the required questions as H3 with bracketed
prompts for the LEDE answer underneath. This forces the FAQ-shape on
generation.

**Scripted answers required where the corpus said so.** Three places in
the skeleton explicitly require *scripts* (actual quoted language), not
guidance:
- The "too expensive" pricing response
- The "competitor X already does this" response
- Each objection in the objection-handling section

**"Awkward questions" are explicit, not implicit.** The skeleton names them:
"What does it NOT do?" / "Who is NOT a good fit?" / "Where do competitors
have a legitimate advantage?" / "What's the do-nothing case?" Each is a
required H3, not optional. This is the corpus's strongest critique of
typical launch FAQs and the structural fix is to require the questions.

**Inherited inputs surfaced as pre-work.** The skeleton has a Step 0 that
checks: positioning_statement exists? battlecard exists? pricing_page exists?
If not, the FAQ will still generate but with a flag noting the answers are
loose pending those upstream artifacts.

**No "why this question matters" preambles.** Smart Brevity's WHY IT MATTERS
is collapsed because every Q is by definition a real-rep question. Stakes
are implicit.

## System prompt failure modes (negative guidance)

Distilled from book + corpus to 8 rules — high concentration because this
artifact's failure modes are unusually specific and pervasive in real PMM
output:

1. **Marketing copy as answers.** Press-release prose, hero-headline lines,
   value-proposition paragraphs in place of operational rep answers.
2. **Missing the awkward questions.** Skipping "what does it NOT do",
   "who is NOT a good fit", "where do competitors have a legitimate
   advantage", "what's the do-nothing case."
3. **Principles instead of scripts** in pricing-objection / competitor-
   objection sections. "Explain your value" is not an answer; quoted
   language is.
4. **Roadmap-as-shipped.** Forward-looking statements written as if
   features are live. Creates legal exposure and rep mistrust when
   prospects ask follow-ups.
5. **Vague SE handoff** ("when it gets technical"). Specific trigger
   required.
6. **Vague discount authority.** "Talk to your manager" is not an answer.
   Name the approver and the threshold.
7. **No known issues section** — or a sanitized one. Reps who get
   blindsided in the field become internal critics.
8. **Long answers.** Each LEDE caps at 2-4 sentences; GO DEEPER caps at
   3-5 bullets. Anything longer is a different artifact.

Voice rule: Reference Smart Brevity / Sales Pitch implicitly. Do not
name-drop authors in the output. Reps don't care which book the
methodology came from.
