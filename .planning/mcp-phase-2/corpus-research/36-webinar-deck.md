## What the books establish (the architecture)

### Sparkline (Resonate) — applied to a 45-min webinar

The webinar is structurally a Sparkline, but compressed: opening (what is) →
problem reframe (what is, deepening) → market POV / new game (what could be,
named) → solution principles (what could be, owned) → product reveal (what
could be, made tangible) → customer story (what could be, made real) → Q&A
(what could be, pressure-tested) → CTA (cross the threshold).

Critically, the product reveal lives in the **last 30%** of the talk —
roughly minute 25-35 of a 45-min webinar. The first 50% is vendor-agnostic
education. This is the line that separates a webinar from a sales pitch deck
(artifact 19) or a customer-launch deck (artifact 14).

### Audience-as-hero / mentor stance (Resonate)

The registrant is the hero. The presenter is the mentor. Webinars fail this
test the moment the deck pivots to "we built", "our platform", "our team".
The body of the webinar lives in the registrant's reality, with the product
mentioned as one possible answer to the question the audience is now asking.

### Idea Worth Spreading (TED)

One takeaway the audience can repeat at the watercooler the next morning.
For a webinar this is *not* a product line — it is a market POV or framework
the audience can cite even if they never become a customer. The takeaway is
the long-tail demand-gen asset; the product reveal is the short-tail.

### Time discipline + slide minimalism (TED)

Webinars are 30-60 min. Most B2B SaaS webinars run 45 min: 30-35 min content
+ 10-15 min Q&A. Slides should be image-led, one idea per slide. Reading the
slide kills the talk.

### S.T.A.R. moment (Resonate)

One Something They'll Always Remember beat. For a webinar, the S.T.A.R. is
most often (a) the customer-story climax (specific number, specific human)
or (b) the demo reveal moment (the "oh, that's it" frame). Without a planted
S.T.A.R., the webinar is informational, not memorable, and the conversion
rate to demo / trial collapses.

## Corpus citations (top 10)

The corpus returned a clean, structured spec. Highlights:

1. **Webinars fail in the middle, not the ends.** "The hook lands, the Q&A
   is fine, but somewhere between minute 8 and minute 35, the audience
   quietly tabs away. Structure is what keeps them."
2. **The opening hook is a specific, uncomfortable truth from the audience's
   own week.** Not housekeeping. Not "About Us". Not "Today we're going to
   talk about…". A reframing stat or question, paired with a *teased
   payoff*: "By the end of this, you'll have a framework you can use in your
   next pipeline review."
3. **The attention curve is non-linear.** Drops hard at minute 8-10, recovers
   if given something new, drops again at minute 25. Design for it: pattern
   interrupt at minute 10 (poll, provocative question, surprising stat).
4. **Demo as proof, not as tour.** The corpus is sharp here: "You've spent
   12 minutes establishing the problem. Now the demo answers one question:
   What does solving it actually look like?" Show one workflow, not a feature
   parade. Build around the moment that creates the "oh, that's it" reaction.
5. **Q&A that converts is seeded.** 2-3 questions seeded with a champion or
   colleague. Real objections (implementation complexity, pricing, "how is
   this different from what we already have") — the questions buyers are
   afraid to ask publicly. Close Q&A with a specific transition, not "feel
   free to reach out".
6. **CTA appears 3x:** end of main content (before Q&A), during Q&A, in
   follow-up email. Most teams only do it once at the very end when half the
   audience is gone.
7. **Reduce the perceived cost of the next step.** "This is a 25-minute
   conversation, not a sales pitch. We'll look at your specific workflow and
   tell you honestly whether this fits." Lower bar than "schedule a demo".
8. **Interactive / async leave-behinds.** A recorded demo the buyer can
   navigate themselves, share with their team, revisit at 11pm when they're
   actually deciding. (Upland Software cited 3x deal-velocity improvement.)
9. **Failure modes named in the corpus:** opening agenda slide, 45-min
   monologue, demo as feature tour, weak CTA, no follow-up sequence. The
   single biggest upgrade: "treat the webinar as the beginning of a
   conversation, not the conversation itself."
10. **Duarte — Sales Pitch (page 129)** surfaces alongside webinar guidance,
    confirming the boundary: a webinar mixes thought-leadership + soft
    product reveal; a sales pitch is for one named champion in a deal cycle.

Sources: 7 PMA blogs + 1 Steli Efti book (Product Demos that Sell, p.35) + 1
April Dunford book (Sales Pitch, p.129) + 1 untitled blog. Strong PMA
density — webinar craft is a well-trodden practitioner topic.

## Sibling boundary (against artifacts 14, 19, 24)

| Sibling | Question it answers | Audience | Setting |
|---------|---------------------|----------|---------|
| **14 — Customer launch deck** | "Why now (about this product)?" | Customers + prospects at a launch event | One-to-many event broadcast (25-30 min virtual / ~40 min in-person) |
| **19 — Sales pitch deck** | "Why us, over the alternatives this champion could choose today, with a specific next step?" | One named champion in a deal cycle | 1:1 sales call (30-60 min) |
| **24 — Executive keynote** | "Why this shift?" | Conference / customer event / all-hands attendees | One-to-many at scale, story-led, performance (18-45 min) |
| **36 — Webinar deck (this artifact)** | "Here's how the game has changed and what it means for your week — and (last 30%) here's one way we help" | Registered demand-gen audience: mostly prospects, some customers, some randoms | Live broadcast with slides + demo + Q&A (30-60 min, typically 45) |

The key axis: a webinar is **demand-gen / education with a soft product
reveal in the last 30%**. The first 50% must be vendor-agnostic enough that
a registrant who never becomes a customer still walks away with one usable
framework. That's what makes the registration list grow over time.

## Design decisions

1. **Skeleton = section-by-section, not slide-by-slide.** Unlike artifacts
   14 and 19 (deck specs), a webinar mixes prose talk track + slides + demo
   + Q&A. Sections map cleanly to the attention curve (0-3 / 3-12 / 12-25 /
   25-35 / 35-45 min). Within each section we name the slide titles and
   the verbal beats — not full word-for-word script (that's artifact 21).

2. **Pre-work locks 4 decisions before drafting:** webinar type (problem-led
   education / customer-marketing case-study / soft-launch reveal), 1 named
   registrant persona, the one takeaway you want them to repeat, and the
   single-CTA framing (low-friction first ask). Pre-work is the discipline
   that prevents feature-tour drift.

3. **Product reveal is structurally constrained to the last 30%.** Slide
   titles and bracket prompts enforce: no product mention before minute 25.
   Earlier mentions collapse the demand-gen frame.

4. **Demo is proof, not tour.** One workflow. One "oh, that's it" moment.
   The skeleton names the moment in pre-work and reinforces it in the demo
   slot.

5. **Seeded Q&A.** Skeleton includes a "seeded questions" pre-work item:
   2-3 real objections seeded with champion/colleague. The Q&A intro
   addresses the seeded answer first (don't end with "any questions?" —
   give the seeded answer first).

6. **CTA stairs, not single CTA.** Three asks at decreasing friction:
   primary (book a 25-min working session, not a "demo"), secondary (try /
   self-serve trial / async demo), tertiary (next webinar in series, gated
   asset, follow-up email opt-in). Stairs let registrants self-select.

7. **Negative system prompt encodes the 5 corpus failure modes verbatim:**
   no logos / agenda / "thank you for joining" open, no pitching in the
   first 50%, no demo as feature tour, no dodging product-fit until Q&A,
   no ending with "any questions?" — give the seeded answer first.

8. **Three-CTA-touch rule** baked into the validation gate: end of main
   content, during Q&A, follow-up email. Most teams only do it once.

9. **Attention-curve audit gate.** Validation includes a "minute 10 pattern
   interrupt" check — if the script has a 10-minute monologue with no
   poll, question, or stat, the middle will collapse.

## Open questions for audit

1. **Should the skeleton render slide-by-slide or section-by-section?**
   Chose section-by-section because the attention curve is the load-bearing
   structure for a webinar (more than the deck itself). Slide titles are
   named inside sections. Audit decision.

2. **Should the async / interactive leave-behind be inside this template,
   or a separate artifact?** The corpus surfaces async-demo (Upland 3x
   velocity) as a real lever. For now the skeleton mentions it as a CTA
   tier; it does not generate a full async-demo spec. Could be a future
   companion artifact.

3. **Does the seeded-Q&A pre-work need a worked example?** The bracket
   prompt names the discipline; the LLM may produce generic seeded questions
   without one. Audit decision: add an example, or trust pre-work clarity.

4. **How aggressive is the "no product before minute 25" rule?** A
   soft-launch-reveal webinar (one of the three webinar types in pre-work)
   may legitimately tease the product earlier. The skeleton allows the
   pre-work to specify webinar type and adjusts the bracket prompts; if
   audit deems this too loose, we tighten.
