## What the canon establishes

### The boundary calls (what makes this NOT artifact 16)

A solo battlecard answers: *we vs. one competitor, generally, in our category.*
A co-sell battlecard answers: *we-with-this-partner vs. competitors-of-the-
combined-motion, in deals where both products are in scope.* Three structural
differences flow from that:

1. **Joint wedge, not solo wedge.** The "How we win" paragraph names the
   outcome neither vendor produces alone. If the wedge survives a "could the
   customer get this with just our product? just the partner's?" test, it's
   real. If either is yes, sharpen the wedge.

2. **Competitors of the combined motion, not competitors of either product.**
   Three categories: *integrated alternative* (a single vendor who bundles
   what we two do), *DIY / build-it-yourself* (their internal team + open
   source), *do nothing / delay* (often the real competitor in complex joint
   deals). This is a different competitor list than either solo battlecard.

3. **Partner economics as a structural section, not a footnote.** A partner
   rep who doesn't know how they get paid won't prioritize the deal. Comp
   structure, deal-reg mechanics, deal qualification (co-sell vs. referral
   vs. resell), and timeline expectations are non-negotiable inclusions.

### What artifact 16 (battlecard) gives us, kept and adapted

- **Wedge-first ordering.** Header → wedge → talk track → trap-setting →
  competitor pitch / strengths / weaknesses → objection handling → do-not-say
  → customer proof → metadata. Same sequence; every section now joint-aware.
- **Leak-tolerance CRITICAL framing.** Co-sell battlecards are MORE
  leak-prone than solo battlecards because they sit in two CRMs, two
  partner portals, and travel between two field orgs. Calibrate as if the
  partner-of-partner sees it (e.g., a competitor who is *also* a partner of
  the partner — hyperscalers all sit on each other's marketplaces).
- **Do-not-say list.** Two-sided now: things we don't say about competitors
  AND things we don't say about the partner that could damage the partnership.
- **One customer proof, full deal context.** One co-sell win with both
  products in production beats a logo wall.
- **Metadata block.** Owner on BOTH sides (our PMM/AM + partner PMM/AM),
  refresh cadence, format, change triggers.

### What's net-new vs. artifact 16

- **Joint customer profile** (replaces solo ICP/deal-context line). The
  account where *both* products create compounding value, owned by a buyer
  who owns *both* problems, with a trigger event that makes both urgent at
  once (migration, audit, scale event, M&A). Plus an explicit **negative
  joint profile** — accounts where only one product fits (running co-sell
  there poisons the partnership).

- **Joint value prop / wedge** (replaces solo wedge). VBF applied to the
  combined offering. Tested with the can-they-get-this-from-just-one-vendor
  filter.

- **Talk track for the *partner* rep, not our rep.** Calibrated to
  someone who knows their platform cold and ours at ~30% depth. Two modes:
  discovery (3 questions to qualify the joint opportunity) and pitch
  (60-second co-sell narrative they can deliver without our deck).

- **Deal-reg / co-sell motion section.** The mechanics: where to register
  (ACE, Salesforce Partner Community, Crossbeam, Reveal — whatever the
  partner uses), what qualifies as co-sell vs. referral vs. resell, response
  SLAs, partner economics (comp structure on a co-sell win), rules of
  engagement (who owns the customer, who leads commercial).

- **Two-path escalation.** Technical (integration question, POC blocker,
  security review — who, SLA, what to bring) and commercial (stuck deal,
  exec alignment, competitive pressure — named contacts, threshold for
  pulling in field leadership). Named contacts and Slack handles when the
  partnership is mature.

- **Joint owner block.** Co-owned by *our* partner PMM (or AM) and the
  partner's equivalent. A co-sell battlecard with a single-side owner is
  one re-org away from being abandoned.

## Template design decisions

**Authority hierarchy:** Punchy + artifact 16 (battlecard) drive the
structural spine. The synthesized Sherpa response (over a thin corpus) drives
the partner-GTM-specific net-new sections. Where corpus is silent (e.g.,
deal-reg specifics), we encode the structural slot and let the user fill it
from their actual program.

**Inherit artifact 16's leak-tolerance CRITICAL framing — and tighten it.**
Co-sell artifacts move between two field orgs and two partner portals. The
calibration rule: assume a *partner-of-partner* (a competitor who is also a
partner of your partner) reads it. That's a real scenario in hyperscaler
ecosystems.

**One battlecard, one (competitor × partner-pairing).** The MCP tool
generates one card per call. If you're co-selling with three partners
against the same competitor, that's three cards — the wedge is different in
each because the joint solution is different. If you're co-selling with one
partner against three competitors, that's three cards too. Cross-product is
intentional: skipping it is failure mode #1.

**Joint owner block, not single-side owner.** Two named owners with two
review dates. A co-sell battlecard with one owner dies in a re-org.

**Deal-reg as a structural section.** Even when the user doesn't yet have
a co-sell program, the section forces the question. The skeleton renders
the prompts even if the answer is "we don't have one yet" — that's a
finding, not a section to skip.

**Two-path escalation, named contacts.** Generic "contact your partner
manager" language is failure mode #4 in the corpus. The skeleton forces
named contacts (or "TBD — name to be added by [date]" as an explicit gap).

**Three-category competitors-of-the-joint-solution.** Integrated
alternative / DIY / do-nothing. Renders as three named blocks, not a free
list. Without the structure the model defaults to listing our competitors
and the partner's competitors (failure mode #1).

**Talk track for the *partner* rep, not us.** Voice rule encoded in the
section description: 30%-depth rule, no slide deck, partner rep's spoken
register. This is the most distinctive voice constraint.

**Do-not-say list is two-sided.** Things not to say about competitors
(inherited from artifact 16) AND things not to say about the partner that
damage the partnership. The latter is net-new for this artifact.

## Sections excluded

- **Multi-competitor coverage** — that's a comparison-style artifact for
  joint motions; not in scope. One card, one (competitor × partner) pairing.
- **Partner-facing one-pager content** — that's artifact 27 (general partner
  rep skim). Co-sell battlecard is per-(competitor × partner), not general.
- **Buyer-facing joint pitch** — that's a joint solution brief (artifact 29).
  Co-sell battlecard is internal-only for both sides.
- **Pricing detail** — same leak risk as artifact 16. Reference current
  pricing pages.
- **Full ICP definition** — inherits from positioning (01) and ICP (04). The
  joint customer profile is a deal-context summary, not a fresh ICP build.
- **Win/loss raw data** — inherits from artifact 07. The card surfaces
  patterns, not transcripts.

## System prompt failure modes (negative guidance)

1. **Solo-product battlecard reused for co-sell.** The wedge, ICP, and
   competitor list are all joint-first. A co-sell card that reads like our
   solo card with the partner's logo bolted on fails on arrival.
2. **No joint wedge — describing each product side-by-side.** "We do X, they
   do Y, together we cover both" is not a wedge. The wedge is the outcome
   neither vendor delivers alone.
3. **Solo competitors listed instead of competitors of the combined motion.**
   The right list: integrated alternative, DIY, do-nothing.
4. **Missing partner economics.** A partner rep who doesn't know how they
   get paid won't prioritize the deal. Comp + deal-reg mechanics are
   non-negotiable.
5. **Missing co-sell motion / deal-reg mechanics.** "Contact your partner
   manager" is not a motion. Where to register, what qualifies, response SLA.
6. **Generic escalation ("contact your partner manager").** Two paths,
   named contacts, thresholds — or explicit "TBD" markers.
7. **Talk track in our register, not the partner rep's.** Partner rep knows
   their platform cold and ours at ~30%. Talk track must be deliverable
   without our slide deck.
8. **Logo-wall joint customer proof.** One specific co-sell win with both
   products in production — industry, size, what they switched from, how
   the joint motion closed it, the joint outcome.
9. **Leakable language about the partner.** Calibrate as if the
   partner-of-partner reads it. No partner-disparaging asides ("we cover the
   gaps in their product"). Do-not-say list is two-sided.
10. **Stale data treated as fresh.** Both products' pricing, packaging, and
    program rules go stale. Date every factual claim.

Voice rule: Reference Punchy / Dunford / artifact 16 implicitly. Do not
name-drop authors or sibling artifacts in the output.

## Open questions for audit

- **Should the deal-reg section refuse to render when the user hasn't yet
  built a co-sell program?** Currently it renders as prompts the user fills
  with "TBD — co-sell program in design as of [date]". Argument for refusing:
  forces the user to establish the program first. Argument against (chosen):
  the prompts surface the gap, which is itself useful PMM output.
- **Should "competitors of the joint solution" be rendered as 3 fixed
  categories or a free list?** Locked at 3 categories (integrated /
  DIY / do-nothing) because corpus and synthesis converge there. Free list
  defaults to solo competitors — failure mode #1.
- **Joint owner block — should both owners be required fields or one
  required and one optional?** Currently both required (with explicit
  "name to be added by [date]" allowed for either). A single-owner card is
  one re-org from death.
- **Should the trap-setting questions be locked at 3 (matching artifact 16)
  or expanded for joint context?** Locked at 3. Same discipline rationale:
  partner reps cannot memorize a long list, and partner reps know our
  product less than our reps do — fewer questions, sharper questions.
- **Corpus gap (logged):** no partner-GTM canon (channel books,
  hyperscaler co-sell program docs, Crossbeam / Reveal practitioner content)
  surfaced. The structural decisions came from synthesis over a thin corpus.
  Should ingest channel-PMM material in next sweep.
