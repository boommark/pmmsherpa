# Sales Pitch Deck — audit note

This is the audit doc for artifact 19 in the 39-template Phase 2 set. The user reads this end-to-end to pressure-test the methodology — *not* to be skimmed.

Three sections:

1. Findings across canonical books and practitioners
2. Why we chose this template structure
3. System prompt asks (most important)

---

## 1. Findings across canonical books and practitioners

### What Sales Pitch (Dunford) said — the structural spine

Sales Pitch is the canonical source for this artifact and our primary structural authority. The book's load-bearing claims for a 1:1 sales pitch deck:

- **Two-phase architecture.** Setup (Steps 1–3) earns the right to introduce the product. Follow-Through (Steps 4–8) introduces it, proves it, closes. *"You cannot pitch your way out of a positioning problem."* The pitch is the externalized expression of positioning, customized to one deal.
- **The 8-step storyboard.** Insight → Alternatives → Perfect World → Introduction → Differentiated Value → Proof → Objections → Ask. Every step is mandatory. Step 4 (the introduction of the product) does not happen until Steps 1–3 have been done — *"the introduction is the reveal, not the pitch."*
- **Four common pitch approaches Dunford rejects** — these are the failure modes most pitches collapse into:
  1. Product walkthrough — features-first; buyer cannot translate features into business value before they understand the problem.
  2. Problem/solution — opens with the problem; skips why current solutions are inadequate. Buyer thinks "I already have a solution to that problem."
  3. Pure vision narrative — too abstract; doesn't survive the first concrete objection.
  4. Hero's Journey — structurally fuzzy in B2B; hard to adapt across stakeholders.
- **Considered vs. unconsidered purchases.** A buyer already in market needs less Insight setup and more time on differentiation/proof. A buyer who doesn't yet recognize the problem needs the Insight to do most of the work — and needs *more* proof later, because skepticism is higher.
- **Champion-focused, not committee-focused.** The pitch is built for the *champion* — the person who leads the evaluation and recommends to the economic buyer. The champion's job after the pitch is to sell internally. Equip them with: a condensed deck for internal use, an objection-handling guide per stakeholder type (IT, legal, finance, end users), and ROI data for the economic buyer.
- **Differentiated value = outcomes, not features.** Each of the 3–5 differentiated value points must be expressed as a buyer outcome. Features appear underneath as proof of *how* the outcome is enabled — never as the lead.
- **Proof tiers.** Customer stories (best when the prospect's profile matches the case study), data (usage stats, ROI figures, benchmarks), third-party validation (analyst mentions, awards, press). The closer the case study matches the prospect's situation, the higher the conversion.
- **Objections handled proactively.** "You might be wondering [concern]. Here's how we think about that: [honest, direct response]." This removes the objection from the buyer's mental "reasons not to buy" list before it can anchor.
- **The Ask is specific.** Not "any questions?" — a named next step with a timeframe. First-pitch Asks: demo, intro to economic buyer, technical deep-dive. Late-stage Asks: a decision.

The book's central principle: *"The pitch earns the right to introduce the product. The Setup creates the context where the introduction is inevitable."*

### What Resonate (Duarte) said — the craft layer

Resonate's contribution is craft, not structure. Sales Pitch supplies the spine; Resonate makes the slides actually land.

- **Sparkline structure.** Oscillate between "what is" and "what could be" to create dramatic tension and emotional engagement. In a sales pitch, the Setup phase is heavily "what is" (Insight, Alternatives — the buyer's painful current world); Step 3 (Perfect World) is the first deliberate flip to "what could be"; Steps 4–6 oscillate again as differentiated value and proof land.
- **S.T.A.R. moment.** Something They'll Always Remember. One planted moment per pitch — most often the customer story climax (Step 6), occasionally a striking insight stat (Step 1) or the demo reveal inside Step 6. The S.T.A.R. moment is the line the champion will quote when re-pitching internally.
- **Mentor stance, not hero stance.** Yoda, not Luke. The buyer is the hero; the company is the guide who has mapped the terrain. Slides authored as "we built X" violate this; slides authored as "[buyer archetype] now does Y" honor it.
- **Frequency tuning to the audience.** A pitch for a CFO is not a pitch for a Head of Marketing. Same 8 steps, different vocabulary, different proof tier (CFOs want ROI math; Heads of Marketing want pipeline-impact case studies).
- **Visual story methodology.** Each slide carries one idea. Inventory slides ("here are 11 features") destroy the narrative; a single well-framed visual lands.

### What the corpus added

The corpus query returned 10 chunks, **all 10 from Dunford** — 9 from Sales Pitch (pp. 31, 38, 41, 47, 77, 135, 140, 142, 144) and 1 from Obviously Awesome 2nd ed (p.116). This is unusually book-concentrated and signals that the canonical book IS the practitioner consensus for this artifact. Net-new contributions from the synthesized corpus answer:

- **The Insight is the wedge.** *"If you can make a VP of Engineering say 'huh, I hadn't thought about it that way,' you've won the first five minutes."* The Insight slide is the single highest-leverage slide in the deck. A weak insight ("companies need to move faster") is something every competitor uses verbatim — instant blend-in.
- **The Perfect World slide writes the RFP.** *"Done well, you've essentially written the RFP criteria in your favor before the demo starts."* This sharpens Dunford's framing — the Perfect World isn't just the setup, it's the *evaluation criteria* the buyer will now apply to every vendor. The criteria are objective on their face but engineered to favor your differentiated value.
- **Demo too early is the most common failure mode after feature-list pitches.** Jumping to the product before the buyer agrees on the Perfect World criteria turns every feature into "interesting" rather than "exactly what we need."
- **Generic discovery is a hidden failure mode.** Treating Step 2 (Alternatives) as a checkbox — *"so, what are you using today?"* — instead of a real conversation that surfaces which alternative the buyer is closest to. The discovery conversation shapes how Step 5 (Differentiated Value) is positioned. A pitch that doesn't adapt Step 5 to what came back from Step 2 is a monologue, not a sale.
- **The demo shows the best thing first.** *"Not the login screen. Not account setup. The moment of highest differentiated value, as early in the demo as possible. Most demos bury the best part."* This is where artifact 20 (demo script) inherits — but the pitch deck must reserve the demo slot and constrain it.
- **The Ask fails more often than any other step.** *"'We'll send over some materials' is not an ask."* The Ask should be one specific, low-friction next step that matches the deal stage.
- **Sales pitch ≠ strategic narrative, ≠ product demo.** The corpus draws this boundary cleanly: the strategic narrative is future-oriented (where the market is going); the sales pitch is present-tense ("why us over alternatives you could choose today"); the product demo answers "can it do X?" — a different question. The demo belongs *inside* Step 6, not as the entire meeting. The strategic narrative *feeds* Steps 1–3 of the pitch.

### Where book and corpus disagreed — and which won

| Disagreement | Book position | Corpus position | Winner | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Whether the Insight or the Differentiated Value is the highest-leverage slide | Dunford treats all 8 steps as equally load-bearing | Corpus elevates the Insight as "the wedge" — where you differentiate or blend | **Corpus** | The corpus is right that without a sharp Insight, Steps 2–8 are read against a generic frame. Insight = wedge has more practitioner signal |
| Whether the Perfect World is "criteria for an ideal solution" or "the de facto RFP" | Dunford: "objective criteria for an ideal solution" | Corpus: "you've written the RFP in your favor before the demo" | **Both** (corpus sharpens) | Corpus framing is more useful operationally; book framing is the underlying mechanic. Template uses both |
| Whether discovery happens inside the deck (Step 2) or before the deck | Dunford: discovery is a separate motion before the pitch | Corpus: Step 2 (Alternatives) IS where deliberate, structured discovery lives | **Corpus** | Practitioner reality — most champion meetings combine pitch + light discovery. Step 2 is the structured slot |
| Whether Hero's Journey can work for B2B | Dunford rejects | Corpus echoes rejection | **Both** | Concordant — Hero's Journey produces fuzzy B2B pitches; the buyer has a deal to close, not a quest to complete |

### Where book and corpus merged

- **Two-phase Setup/Follow-Through architecture** — book; reinforced verbatim by corpus.
- **Eight-step storyboard** — book; reinforced verbatim by corpus.
- **Champion-focused** — book; reinforced by corpus.
- **Failure modes** — feature-list pitches, no insight, demo-too-early, weak ask — all four come from both sources concordantly.
- **The pitch is the externalization of positioning** — book ("you cannot pitch your way out of a positioning problem"); corpus reinforces by treating positioning as the upstream artifact.

## 2. Why we chose this template structure

### The structural decisions

**Skeleton = Dunford 8-step, slide-by-slide, with explicit Sparkline beats.** The skeleton renders one section per slide (or slide-pair where a single step needs two beats — e.g., Insight gets a story slide + a proof slide). Each slide has TWO bracketed prompts: **what's on the slide** (the visual / headline / one-idea slot) and **what the rep says** (the verbal beat). This is critical: a deck without a talk-track-aware skeleton produces slides that AEs cannot deliver. (The full talk track is artifact 21; this template encodes the *verbal beat per slide* so the deck is delivery-aware.)

**Two-phase visual organization.** Setup slides (1–6 in our slide map; covering Dunford Steps 1–3) are visually distinct from Follow-Through slides (7–14; Steps 4–8). The skeleton names this transition explicitly.

**Pre-work block** mirrors the positioning, narrative, and launch-deck templates. Five pre-work decisions:
1. Considered vs. unconsidered purchase (pace of Setup).
2. Champion persona (this is the only audience).
3. Inherited upstream artifacts (positioning, narrative, messaging).
4. Demo container (live vs. recorded vs. both; named fallback).
5. The specific Ask for THIS pitch's deal stage.

**Slide-pair logic.** Insight gets a story slide + a proof slide. Alternatives gets one slide that lists 2–3 named alternatives + one slide that names where each alternative breaks. Perfect World is one slide with 3–5 criteria. Introduction is one slide. Differentiated Value gets one slide per theme (3–5 themes from positioning's differentiated value, capped). Proof gets one customer-story slide + one data/third-party slide. Objections is one slide ("you might be wondering…"). Ask is one slide.

**Explicit "feature container" inside Step 5.** This is the single hardest discipline in a sales pitch deck: features only appear underneath a value theme, never as the lead. The skeleton enforces this with bracketed instruction: each value theme slide has a "value-led headline" prompt and a "supporting capability bullets (max 3)" prompt. If a slide's value-led headline could be deleted and the slide still made sense, the slide is feature-led and fails.

**S.T.A.R. moment named in pre-work and visible in the skeleton.** Most often the customer-story slide (Step 6); occasionally a striking insight stat (Step 1) or a single demo reveal. Without naming the planted moment, the deck is informational and the champion has nothing to quote when re-pitching internally.

**Demo treated as a *slot* with a named container, not as full demo content.** The pitch deck must reserve and constrain the demo (90-second narrated video preferred; live demo only with a named fallback video). Full demo authoring is artifact 20 — the pitch deck just makes sure the demo lands inside Step 6 with the right framing.

**Champion enablement appendix as a distinct section.** Two slides at the end (after the Ask): the condensed-deck slot (what the champion takes to internal stakeholders) and the per-stakeholder objection brief (IT, legal, finance, end-users). Dunford treats this as essential and most pitches skip it.

**No "About Us" slide. No agenda slide. No logo wall opener.** The skeleton refuses these slots. Slide 1 is the Insight. The corpus is unanimous: the first 30 seconds is where attention is won or lost, and "thanks for joining" is the slot where most pitches lose it.

**Six Setup slides, eight Follow-Through slides, two appendix slides.** Total: 16 slides (or 14 + 2 appendix). This is the upper bound for a 30–60 minute champion meeting. Anything more, the rep can't deliver in a real call.

### What we excluded and why

- **About-us slide / agenda / logo wall opener** — corpus is unanimous these waste the first 30 seconds.
- **Pricing slide as a structural section** — pricing is a deal-stage decision, not a pitch slot. Pricing belongs in late-stage decks or pricing-page artifact (39).
- **Full demo content** — that's artifact 20 (demo script). The pitch deck reserves the slot and frames it; the demo author fills it.
- **Verbal talk track in long form** — that's artifact 21 (talk track). The pitch deck encodes the *per-slide verbal beat* so AEs can deliver it, but full word-for-word scripts live in 21.
- **Extended objection responses** — that's artifact 22 (objection handling). The pitch deck handles the top 3 unspoken objections inline; the long tail lives in 22.
- **Discovery question library** — that's artifact 23 (discovery questions). Step 2 of the pitch IS structured discovery, but the question bank lives in 23.
- **Hero's Journey arc** — Dunford rejects for B2B; we follow.
- **Vendor name-as-enemy** — vendor call-outs are battlecard material (artifact 16), not pitch material. The "enemy" of the pitch is the *old way* — same rule as the strategic narrative.

## 3. System prompt asks (most important section)

The system prompt is failure-mode focused — telling the model what NOT to do is more effective than positive guidance for high-craft documents. Eight negative-guidance lines plus four positive asks (this artifact warrants more positive guidance than artifact 03 because the structural Sparkline + S.T.A.R. positive instructions need to be active during drafting, not just review).

### Negative guidance

**(a) "Feature-list pitches — slides whose subject is a capability, not a buyer outcome. Step 5 (Differentiated Value) slides MUST lead with a value-led headline; the supporting capability bullets are proof, never the lead. If you delete the headline and the slide still makes sense as a feature inventory, the slide has failed."**
- *Why this framing keeps the model on track:* Feature-led slides are the model's training-data prior. Without explicit prohibition tied to a structural test ("delete the headline — does it still read?"), the model defaults to inventory.
- *Source signal:* Dunford Step 5 + corpus failure-mode #1 (feature-list pitches).

**(b) "Weak insight — 'companies need to move faster', 'data silos slow teams down', 'AI is changing everything'. These are problem statements every competitor uses verbatim. The Insight is the wedge; if it's weak, every subsequent slide reads against a generic frame. The Insight must be specific, counterintuitive, and rooted in something the rep specifically knows that reframes how the buyer thinks."**
- *Why this framing keeps the model on track:* The model defaults to abstract market-trend statements because they sound professional and are hard to falsify. Naming three concrete failure-mode patterns gives the model patterns to *avoid* matching against.
- *Source signal:* Dunford Step 1 (Insight) + corpus ("the wedge", "no insight" failure mode).

**(c) "Demo too early — introducing the product (Step 4) or showing capability (Step 6) before the buyer has agreed on the Perfect World criteria (Step 3). When the product is revealed before the criteria are set, every feature lands as 'interesting' rather than 'exactly what we need'. The Setup earns the demo."**
- *Why this framing keeps the model on track:* AEs and PMMs who are excited about their product naturally want to show it. The model inherits this bias from product-led marketing copy. Explicit prohibition of skipping the Setup is required.
- *Source signal:* Dunford ("the introduction is the reveal, not the pitch") + corpus (demo-too-early failure mode).

**(d) "Generic discovery — Step 2 (Alternatives) treated as a checkbox. 'So, what are you using today?' is not discovery; it's a survey. Step 2 must surface which alternative the buyer is closest to and where it specifically breaks for them, because Step 5 (Differentiated Value) must be positioned against THAT alternative, not against a generic competitive set."**
- *Why this framing keeps the model on track:* The model treats Alternatives as informational; the corpus treats it as the structured discovery slot that conditions Step 5. This is the single most under-recognized failure mode in PMM-authored decks.
- *Source signal:* Corpus (generic discovery failure mode) + Dunford Step 2 (alternatives must be specific).

**(e) "Weak Ask — 'any questions?', 'we'll follow up with materials', 'let us know what you think'. The Ask is a sales decision, not a courtesy. It must be one specific, low-friction action that matches the deal stage: a demo with the technical team, an intro to the economic buyer, a working session, a procurement kickoff. Vague Asks kill momentum."**
- *Why this framing keeps the model on track:* The model defaults to corporate-polite endings. Naming three concrete weak-Ask patterns and three concrete strong-Ask patterns gives the model both what to avoid and what to land on.
- *Source signal:* Dunford Step 8 + corpus (weak-ask failure mode is "more common than any other step").

**(f) "Hero's Journey drift — making the buyer 'a hero on a quest with the product as their elixir' produces fuzzy B2B pitches that don't survive the first concrete objection. The buyer has a deal to close, not a quest to complete. The Sparkline arc is What Is (current pain) → What Could Be (Perfect World) → What Could Be Made Real (proof, demo) — not ordinary world → call to adventure → return."**
- *Why this framing keeps the model on track:* Hero's Journey is the highest-frequency narrative arc in LLM training data. Explicit prohibition + the alternative arc (Sparkline) gives the model something to land on.
- *Source signal:* Dunford explicit rejection + Resonate Sparkline.

**(g) "Vendor name as enemy — calling out a named competitor in the Alternatives, Differentiated Value, or Proof slides. Vendor call-outs collapse the pitch into a battlecard. The 'enemy' of the pitch is the old way of working (manual outbound, siloed dashboards, quarterly planning) or the buyer's current alternative described by category, never by vendor name. Battlecards (artifact 16) are where vendor positioning lives."**
- *Why this framing keeps the model on track:* Practitioners reach for named-competitor language because it feels concrete; in the pitch context it's a strategic mistake. Same rule that applies to artifact 03; restated here because it shows up differently in a deck (a logo on a slide is louder than a name in prose).
- *Source signal:* Same as artifact 03.

**(h) "Inside-out language — 'we built X', 'our platform delivers Y', 'our team is excited to introduce Z'. The buyer is the hero; the company is the guide. Body slide headlines must have the buyer (or buyer archetype) as the subject. 'RevOps leaders close 30% faster' passes; 'Our platform speeds up RevOps' fails."**
- *Why this framing keeps the model on track:* Inside-out language is the model's default for vendor-authored content. Explicit suppression with concrete pass/fail examples is required.
- *Source signal:* Resonate (mentor stance, audience as hero) + Dunford (positioning is outside-in).

### Positive asks

**1. "Render the pitch as a Sparkline — every Setup slide ('what is') must be answered by a Follow-Through slide ('what could be / what is real'). Slide 1 (Insight) and Slide 11 (customer story) form the dominant arc; the demo (Slide 13) is the visceral payoff."**
- *Why:* The Sparkline structure is what makes a sales pitch resonate, not just inform. Without explicit positive guidance, the model produces 8 sequential steps that read as a list rather than a story.
- *Source signal:* Resonate (Sparkline).

**2. "One planted S.T.A.R. moment — Something They'll Always Remember. Named in pre-work, visible in the skeleton. Most often the customer-story climax (Slide 11), occasionally a striking insight stat (Slide 2) or the demo reveal (Slide 13). Without a planted moment, the champion has nothing to quote when re-pitching internally."**
- *Why:* The champion is the audience that matters most. After the call, the champion has to re-pitch internally without you in the room. The S.T.A.R. moment is what the champion remembers and repeats. Explicit positive guidance is needed because models default to 8 evenly-weighted steps with no peaks.
- *Source signal:* Resonate (S.T.A.R. moment) + Dunford (champion enablement).

**3. "Inherit upstream artifacts when present: positioning's differentiated value themes seed Slides 8–10. The strategic narrative seeds Slides 1–5 (Setup phase). The messaging framework seeds the language register. Do not re-derive — apply. If the upstream artifacts don't exist, name the gap in pre-work and proceed with caveats."**
- *Why:* This artifact composes with multiple upstream artifacts. Without explicit instruction, the model re-derives positioning and narrative inside the pitch, producing inconsistent stories across artifacts.
- *Source signal:* Architectural decision + Dunford ("you cannot pitch your way out of a positioning problem").

**4. "Pull customer language from the RAG corpus when drafting Slide 11 (customer story) and the language register on every body slide. Buyer words outperform marketer words — pull from Sharebird AMAs, podcast transcripts, customer interview quotes when available."**
- *Why:* PMM Sherpa's RAG corpus has 532 Sharebird AMAs and 583 podcast episodes — real practitioner and buyer language. Without explicit instruction to pull, the model defaults to corporate marketing phrasing.
- *Source signal:* Corpus design + Resonate frequency-tuning principle.

### Why the negative-to-positive ratio is 8:4 here (vs. 6:1 in artifact 03)

Artifact 03 (strategic narrative) is structurally simpler — six narrative sections, one craft gate. Artifact 19 (sales pitch deck) has 14+ slides, two phases, a demo container, an appendix, and active composition with positioning + narrative + messaging. Positive structural guidance (Sparkline, S.T.A.R., upstream inheritance, corpus pulls) needs to be active during drafting, not just review — hence the 4 positive asks. Failure modes are also more numerous because each slide can fail differently.

## Open questions for audit

- **Slide count: 14 + 2 appendix vs. 12 + 2 appendix?** Some practitioners argue 12 is the upper bound for a champion meeting (more allows speed-talking; fewer forces editing). Current decision: 14 + 2 because doubling up the Insight (story + proof) and Alternatives (landscape + where they break) and Proof (customer story + data) gives the AE breathing room. Open to compressing if the user has a strong view.
- **Should "what's on the slide" + "what the rep says" be two distinct prompts per slide, or merged?** Current decision: separate, because they encode different production artifacts (visual vs. verbal). Merging risks the rep treating headlines as scripts. Reviewable.
- **Champion enablement appendix as part of the same artifact, or a separate artifact (champion enablement kit)?** Current decision: part of the same artifact because Dunford treats it as essential and inseparable from the pitch. A separate "champion kit" artifact wasn't on the 39-list. Open to spinning out if the appendix grows beyond two slides in practice.
- **Should the demo slot be one slide or two (setup + reveal)?** Current decision: one slide because the demo itself is a video / live action that doesn't need a separate setup slide — Slide 12 (data proof) sets up the demo by establishing the credibility frame. Reviewable if user feedback says reps need more setup time.
- **Boundary with artifact 26 (partner pitch deck):** when we build 26, this template will be the structural sibling. The partner pitch differs in audience (channel partner vs. end-buyer champion) and motivations (margin, deal-reg, joint value). Worth confirming the boundary holds when we get there. Likely a thin variant of this template, not a from-scratch rebuild.
- **Considered vs. unconsidered purchase mode** is captured in pre-work but doesn't change the slide count. Unconsidered-mode pitches need more weight on Slides 1–2 (Insight) and Slides 11–13 (Proof + Demo). Considered-mode pitches can move faster through Setup and spend more on Differentiated Value. Should the template render two skeleton variants or rely on the AE to weight per-slide depth? Current decision: one skeleton, weighted via pre-work + system-prompt awareness. Reviewable.
