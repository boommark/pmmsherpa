# Phase 2 Audit — 27 Partner Enablement One-Pager

The Partner Enablement One-Pager is the asset a **partner rep** (not their exec,
not their customer) skims for 30 seconds before a customer call, when our
product comes up in their pipeline, or when they're deciding whether to loop
us in. The whole template is built on three constraints: (a) it must be
skim-able in under a minute, (b) it must equip — not persuade — the partner
rep, and (c) it must answer the four questions a partner rep is actually
asking themselves: *what is this thing*, *is this deal a fit*, *what do I
say*, and *who do I call when I'm stuck*.

---

## 1. Findings across canonical books and practitioners

### What Punchy said (the editorial register)

Punchy is not a partner-enablement book — it's a messaging book — but three
of its disciplines map directly onto the partner-rep audience:

- **VBF Rule (Value → Benefit → Feature).** The partner rep is reading this
  to find lines they can repeat in front of a customer. If the doc opens
  with feature-soup ("our platform delivers AI-native, multi-tenant…"), the
  rep cannot recycle a single sentence. The "What we are" line, the
  differentiators, and the use cases all render as Value-first by template
  enforcement.
- **A+ Customer.** Partner-rep enablement collateral is dead-on-arrival when
  it's written for "any partner, any deal". The doc is built for one specific
  joint customer profile — a partner rep who works deals outside that profile
  will still benefit, but the asset's center of gravity is the joint
  best-fit. Stratton's principle that "messaging written for everyone
  resonates with no one" applies even harder to enablement copy because the
  reader has to use it in a real conversation.
- **BBQ Talk.** Every line in this asset must read like something a partner
  rep would actually say to their customer, not like something a marketing
  team approved for a website. This is the single biggest register failure
  Stratton calls out, and the corpus confirms it as failure mode #1 for
  partner enablement specifically.

What Punchy does NOT cover for this artifact:

- The escalation / contact section (a logistics pattern, not a craft pattern).
- Deal registration mechanics (a partner-program detail, not a copy detail).
- The qualifying questions block as a *partner gate-keeping* tool (Punchy's
  framing is buyer-discovery, not partner-disqualification).

### What Smart Brevity said (the skim discipline)

Smart Brevity gives the formatting and density rules:

- **Audience-first.** The doc opens with what the *partner rep* needs (joint
  customer profile, what to say, who to call) before any company-history or
  product-philosophy filler. The Smart Brevity diagnostic is: "would the
  reader, glancing at this for 10 seconds, get the answer to their question?"
  For a partner rep, that question is "should I loop these people into my
  deal?" — and the structure must answer it on the first glance.
- **TEASE → LEDE → WHY IT MATTERS.** The header, the "What we are" line, and
  the "Ideal joint customer" block together constitute the LEDE: by the time
  a rep finishes the first three lines, they know what we are, who we sell
  to, and whether their deal qualifies. Everything below is GO DEEPER.
- **Short, not shallow.** The constraint that the entire asset fits on one
  page (or one screen) forces every section to earn its space. Long
  enablement decks fail this artifact's job because partner reps don't read
  them.
- **Bullets over paragraphs.** Smart Brevity's research-backed rule: skim
  density wins. Use cases, differentiators, and qualifying questions all
  render as bulleted items, not narrative paragraphs.

### What the corpus added (top citations)

The corpus query returned a single synthesized response with 10 citations,
the bulk of them PMA practitioner blogs and AMAs on sales-enablement and
collateral-creation patterns. The synthesized response is unusually clean
— it directly proposes the section ordering and names every failure mode
in the brief — so the audit decisions below cite it heavily.

Top substantive citations:

1. **Devon O'Rourke / PMA — How to build an effective, repeatable GTM process
   (Sales enablement, audience communication, and case studies).** Establishes
   that enablement collateral has a different job from prospect collateral:
   the prospect needs to be persuaded; the rep needs to be equipped. Different
   register, different content, different success metric.
2. **Bryony Pearce / PMA — Your guide to sales enablement (Sales enablement
   assets).** Anatomy of a one-pager-style enablement asset: who-it's-for,
   what-it-is, who-buys, what-to-say, when-to-escalate. Adopted as the
   template's structural backbone for the partner-rep variant.
3. **Stevie Langford / PMA — What is B2B Messaging (Prevalent pitfalls).**
   The pitfall taxonomy maps onto partner enablement directly: jargon,
   inside-out language, generic benefits — all amplified when the reader
   is a partner rep who has zero context on our product.
4. **Stefano Perrotta / PMA — How Aircall aligns PMM and sales enablement
   (Why is sales enablement so challenging?).** Explicit: the gap between
   collateral that PMM ships and collateral that reps actually use is
   enormous; bridging it requires writing for the rep's actual workflow,
   not the rep's ideal workflow.
5. **PMA — Common barriers when launching a product (Lack of in-house
   knowledge/training).** Surfaces the partner-specific version of this
   problem: partner reps have *less* product knowledge than internal reps
   and *less* time to read; the asset has to compensate for both.
6. **Amey Kanade AMA / Jesse Lopez AMA / PMA podcast.** Partner-program and
   sales-enablement practitioner POV. Net-new addition: the qualifying-
   questions section is the *single most consequential* block on the page
   — without it, partner reps either bring you into bad-fit deals or fail
   to bring you into good ones because they're unsure of fit.
7. **PMA — How to create product collateral / Most effective sales assets.**
   Reinforces format discipline: one page, scannable, dated, owned by a
   named PMM.

### Net-new contributions from the corpus the books did not provide

1. **Joint use cases vs. our use cases.** A use case that doesn't include
   the partner's service or context reads as "sell our product for us." The
   corpus is firm: use cases must show where the partner's value-add lands
   alongside ours. This shifts the section from a generic "top 3 use cases"
   to an explicitly **joint** use case block.
2. **Disqualifier line in the joint customer profile.** The corpus surfaced
   this as a high-leverage one-liner: who's a *bad fit*. Saves partner reps
   from wasted cycles, builds trust, and is rarely included in
   marketing-flyer-repurposed enablement assets.
3. **Qualifying questions as the active-pipeline mechanism.** "Without them,
   partner reps bring you into deals that aren't real, or worse, never
   bring you in at all." The qualifying questions are not buyer-discovery
   questions (those live in artifact 23); they are *partner-side fit-
   confirmation* questions the partner rep asks before looping us in.
4. **Escalation contacts as a 3-row table, not free text.** Technical
   support, deal structuring, customer escalation — three named situations,
   each with a person/role and a channel. Free-text "reach out to your
   partner manager" fails the embarrassingly-easy test the corpus calls out.
5. **Deal registration link as a discrete section, not a footer.** Visible,
   with the registration window stated. Partners protect deals only when
   the asset makes registration frictionless.

### Where book and corpus disagreed — and which won

**On register: marketing voice vs. operational voice.** Smart Brevity's
default is journalist-clean; some PMA blogs lean glossy/marketing. The
corpus and Punchy converge: this is an *operational* asset for a partner
rep, not a glossy flyer for a customer.
- **Winner: Punchy + Smart Brevity (book) over PMA marketing-glossy register.**
  The system prompt explicitly bans the "marketing flyer repurposed for
  partners" pattern as failure mode #1.
- *Reason:* the corpus query itself diagnosed this exact pattern as the
  most common partner-enablement failure. The book canon backs the same
  call.

**On length: paragraphs vs. bullets.** Some PMA collateral guides allow
narrative paragraphs in enablement docs. Smart Brevity rules this out for
skim-able assets.
- **Winner: Smart Brevity.** Bullets and short rows throughout. One
  paragraph is allowed only in the "What we are" line (one sentence, not
  a paragraph).

**On differentiator framing: feature-vs-feature comparison vs. value
differentiator.** Some practitioner content frames partner-rep
differentiators as a feature checklist ("we have X, they don't"). Punchy's
VBF rule and the battlecard discipline (artifact 16) both reject this.
- **Winner: Punchy.** Differentiators render as buyer-outcome statements
  with the structural reason why competitors can't match — not a feature
  checkbox.

### Where book and corpus merged

- **Failure-mode list.** Punchy's BBQ Talk + Smart Brevity's fog-of-words +
  PMA's prevalent pitfalls + PMA's enablement-collateral pitfalls combined
  into 8 negative-guidance rules in the system prompt.
- **Joint-best-fit framing.** Punchy's A+ Customer + corpus's joint-customer
  framing merged: the asset is built for one specific *joint* best-fit
  account, not a generic partner-rep audience.
- **Three-section LEDE.** Smart Brevity's LEDE rule + PMA's enablement
  anatomy converge on: header → What we are (one sentence) → Ideal joint
  customer block. Three sections, by the time the partner rep finishes
  them they have the answer to "should I loop this in?"

## 2. Why we chose this template structure

### The structural decisions

**Skeleton order = skim-optimized, not strategic.** Header (LEDE) → What we
are (one sentence, not a paragraph) → Ideal joint customer (with
disqualifier) → Top 3 joint use cases → Top 3 differentiators → Qualifying
questions → Escalation contacts (3-row table) → Deal registration → Footer
metadata. Top-down reading order matches partner-rep workflow order: *what,
who, when (use cases), why us (differentiators), how do I qualify, who
do I call, how do I register*.

**One page or one screen, hard.** Smart Brevity's constraint, enforced as
a system-prompt rule. If the draft exceeds one page, the failure is in
section length, not in section count.

**"What we are" is one sentence, not a value prop block.** Partner reps
are not the audience for the upstream messaging framework's full value
proposition. They need a one-line that they can paraphrase to a customer.
The sentence renders Value → Benefit → category context, in that order.

**Joint customer block = three lines + a disqualifier.** Company profile,
champion, economic buyer, disqualifier. The disqualifier line is the
corpus addition; it saves partner reps' time and builds program trust.

**Three joint use cases, locked.** Mirrors Punchy's three-benefit
discipline (artifact 02 messaging framework) and the corpus pattern. Each
use case explicitly names the partner's role alongside ours — not "what
the customer does with our product" but "what the customer does with
[partner's offering] + our product, jointly".

**Three differentiators, in VBF voice.** Inherited from positioning
(artifact 01) and battlecard (artifact 16). Each differentiator is one
sentence, buyer-outcome led, no checkbox tables.

**Qualifying questions = exactly four.** Three is too thin (corpus says
the questions need to cover pain, authority, fit, *and* competitive
context); five is too many (partner reps won't memorize five). Four is
the minimum that covers the four jobs and the maximum a rep can hold in
working memory.

**Escalation contacts = 3-row table.** Technical support, deal/pricing,
customer escalation. Each row: situation, person/role, channel. Free
text is banned.

**Deal registration = discrete block, not a footer.** Link + the
registration window stated explicitly (e.g., "register within 30 days of
first conversation").

**Footer metadata: PMM owner, partner program owner, last reviewed,
next review.** Partner enablement assets go stale faster than internal
sales assets because partner reps notice staleness more (they're already
skeptical of vendor collateral). Quarterly refresh, dated.

### Trade-offs we accepted

- **No detailed product overview.** This is the upstream "What we are"
  sentence's whole job. Long product overviews are what makes
  marketing-flyer-repurposed enablement fail.
- **No customer logos / proof slide.** Logos belong in the partner pitch
  deck (artifact 26) for execs. The partner rep needs *qualifying
  questions* and *escalation contacts*, not social proof.
- **No competitor comparison.** That's the co-sell battlecard (artifact
  28), which is per-competitor. This asset is general per-product; if a
  partner rep needs head-to-head, they pull battlecard 28.
- **No pricing detail.** Pricing changes faster than this asset can
  refresh. The asset says "deal structuring → contact [name]" and lets
  that channel handle it.
- **No tone-of-voice guidelines.** Brand voice belongs in a brand
  guidelines artifact, not enablement.

### What we excluded and why

- Long product overview — fails Smart Brevity skim discipline.
- Customer logos — belongs in partner pitch deck (26).
- Competitor head-to-head — belongs in co-sell battlecard (28).
- Pricing — refresh-cadence mismatch; route to escalation instead.
- Tone-of-voice — separate artifact.
- Persona empathy maps — that's buyer_persona (artifact 05).
- ROI calculator — separate enablement asset.

## 3. System prompt asks (most important section)

The system prompt is failure-mode-heavy: 8 negative-guidance rules and 4
positive asks. The reason is the same as in artifact 02: for high-craft,
register-sensitive assets, telling the model what *not* to do beats positive
guidance, because the dominant training prior is glossy marketing copy —
exactly the failure mode #1 the corpus calls out.

### Negative-guidance lines (what we tell the AI to avoid)

1. **"Marketing flyer voice. This is NOT a customer-facing prospect asset.
   Persuasion copy ('the leading platform for…', 'transform your business')
   has no place here. The partner rep is being equipped, not persuaded."**
   - *Source:* Corpus (failure mode #1). This is the single most important
     line in the system prompt.

2. **"Inside-out language ('we built X', 'our platform delivers Y'). Partner
   reps cannot recycle inside-out lines in customer conversations. Use
   buyer-outcome language."**
   - *Source:* Punchy (Inside-Out vs Outside-In).

3. **"Generic differentiators ('best-in-class', 'AI-native', 'enterprise-
   grade', 'easier to use'). The competitor-verbatim test: if a direct
   competitor could lift this line word-for-word, it's not a differentiator."**
   - *Source:* Punchy (Competitive Messaging Audit) + corpus.

4. **"Use cases that don't name the partner's role. A use case described
   only in terms of our product reads as 'sell our product for us'.
   Joint use cases must show where the partner's service or context
   provides the value-add alongside us."**
   - *Source:* Corpus (net-new contribution).

5. **"Skipping the disqualifier in the joint customer profile. One line
   on who is NOT a fit saves partner reps from wasted cycles and builds
   program trust."**
   - *Source:* Corpus (net-new contribution).

6. **"Qualifying questions phrased as buyer-discovery questions. These
   are partner-side fit-confirmation questions the rep asks themselves
   or their customer to decide whether to loop us in. They are NOT the
   sales discovery question set (artifact 23)."**
   - *Source:* Corpus + boundary call vs. artifact 23.

7. **"Free-text escalation. 'Reach out to your partner manager' fails
   the embarrassingly-easy test. Render escalation as a 3-row table:
   situation → person/role → channel."**
   - *Source:* Corpus (escalation friction = where joint deals die).

8. **"More than one page. If the draft spills past a single page or
   single screen, cut. Partner reps will not read a multi-page asset
   under time pressure. The constraint is the discipline."**
   - *Source:* Smart Brevity (skim discipline).

### Positive asks (what we tell the AI to do)

1. **"Render exactly three joint use cases and three differentiators —
   not four, not five."**
   - *Source:* Punchy (three-benefit lock); corpus pattern.

2. **"Render exactly four qualifying questions — covering pain, authority,
   fit, competitive context."**
   - *Source:* Corpus (the four jobs the questions must do).

3. **"Inherit positioning's Distinct Capabilities, Differentiated Value,
   and Best-Fit Accounts (artifact 01) as the upstream input. Inherit the
   ICP (artifact 04) as the joint customer firmographic input. If those
   artifacts don't exist, flag it — don't invent positioning here."**
   - *Source:* Architectural decision to compose with upstream artifacts.

4. **"Date every factual claim and name a PMM owner. Partner enablement
   assets go stale faster than internal sales assets; staleness destroys
   partner-rep trust. Quarterly refresh minimum."**
   - *Source:* Corpus (refresh cadence + named ownership).

### Why we structured the system prompt this way (negative > positive)

The dominant training prior for "partner one-pager" is the marketing
flyer — glossy, inside-out, persuasion-coded. Positive guidance ("be
operational, equip the rep") sits alongside that prior without
overriding it. Negative guidance ("do not write persuasion copy; this
is not a flyer") actively suppresses the prior at the token level.

The 8:4 ratio reflects the high concentration of register failure modes
specific to partner enablement (more than for the messaging framework,
because the audience-mismatch trap is sharper here).

## Open questions for audit

- **Should the qualifying questions be exactly 4 or "3-5"?** Currently
  locked at 4. Argument for 3-5: different partner programs have different
  qualification depths. Argument for 4 (chosen): working-memory ceiling
  for a rep mid-call, and the four-jobs framing (pain/authority/fit/
  competitive) is clean.
- **Should the disqualifier live in the joint customer block or be its
  own line?** Currently inside the joint customer block as the fourth
  bullet. Argument for separating: makes it more visible. Argument for
  keeping in the block (chosen): keeps the asset shorter; the block is
  the natural place a partner rep looks for fit signals.
- **Should escalation contacts include a fourth row for legal /
  compliance?** Currently three rows. Most partner programs route
  legal/compliance through the deal-structuring contact. Open for the
  user audit if the partner program has a separate legal channel.
- **Joint use cases: should each one explicitly name the partner type
  (e.g., "for SI partners", "for ISV partners") or stay agnostic?**
  Currently agnostic — the partner program owner customizes per partner
  type. A partner-type-specific variant could be a future artifact.
- **Corpus gap:** none flagged. The corpus query returned a clean,
  citation-rich response that mapped directly onto the brief's failure
  modes. This is one of the cleanest corpus runs in Phase 2 so far.
