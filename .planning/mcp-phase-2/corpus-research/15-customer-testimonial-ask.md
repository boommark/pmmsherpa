## Boundary clarity (resolved before drafting)

Three frames that often get conflated:

- **The ask** (this artifact) — the email / Slack DM / in-person script that *requests* a quote or testimonial from a customer. Short-form. Output: a message you send.
- **The published testimonial** — the resulting quote with attribution, used on a website, deck slide, or sales asset. A downstream artifact (lives inside landing_page_copy, sales_pitch_deck, battlecard, etc.).
- **The case study** (artifact 37) — long-form customer story with situation/intervention/result + quotes embedded. Different artifact, different ask, different commitment level.

This template covers the **ask only**. Composition with the published quote and case-study artifacts is by reference.

## Ask-variant choice (the central design decision)

Real customer-marketing practice runs two distinct asks that share most of the structure but differ on opening, length, and proof-of-relationship:

- **Warm ask** — to a customer with whom you (or a teammate) have a direct, recent relationship signal: a glowing call, a Slack thank-you, a renewal conversation, a review they left elsewhere. Opening cites the specific moment. Conversion rate is high; this is the bread-and-butter ask.
- **Cold(er) ask** — to a customer where the relationship is real but no recent acute signal exists, or you're asking through CSM/AE intermediary, or the quote needs to come from a *different* persona than your champion (e.g., champion is a Manager but the buyer needs a VP-titled quote, so you're asking the champion to broker an intro to their boss). Opening leans on outcome data + brokered context. Conversion rate is lower; the ask works harder.

**Decision: render BOTH variants in the template.** Practitioner sources are unanimous that the warm-ask wins more, but the cold(er) variant is what PMMs actually need help drafting because it's harder to write and easier to get wrong. Folding them into one variant produces a mushy template that fits neither situation. Folding the cold variant into a later section ("if you don't have a recent moment...") buries the harder craft. Two parallel variants with shared anatomy is the cleanest spine.

The template marks the warm-ask as the default (first, and recommended unless a recent specific moment genuinely doesn't exist) so users don't reach for the cold variant when the warm one would land.

## Anatomy (shared across variants)

From the corpus synthesis, the high-leverage anatomy is six elements, in order:

1. **Relationship-grounded opening.** Warm: cite the specific moment ("you mentioned on our call last Tuesday that…"). Cold: cite the specific outcome data + the brokered context ("your team's Q3 reporting time dropped 40% — Sarah suggested I reach out…"). Generic openings ("hope you're well!") are the #1 corpus failure mode.
2. **Specific business outcome you want them to speak to.** Not "your experience with us" — the *exact* number, decision, or shift. This is also the seed of the pre-drafted quote. The ask is sharper when you already know the soundbite.
3. **What you'll use it for, with channel specificity.** "A short quote for our sales deck and homepage" or "a 60-second video for the launch page next month." Customers get nervous when they don't know where their words will end up. Vagueness reads as risk.
4. **Time commitment, named in minutes.** "Five minutes — just react to a draft I've put together" or "fifteen minutes on Zoom for a video clip." The specific number is a green light. Vague time framing ("just a few minutes of your time") feels like a trap.
5. **Pre-drafted quote (the easy-yes mechanic).** The single highest-leverage move in the whole genre. You write a draft quote in *their* voice, grounded in the specific outcome, and ask them to edit it however they'd like. Most customers approve with light tweaks. Asking from scratch is asking for unpaid work; asking them to react to a draft is a cognitive freebie.
6. **Single-question close.** "Does that work?" or "Happy to adjust if it doesn't quite fit." Easy yes/no, low pressure, no compound questions. Multi-question closes ("does this work, and could you also do a video, and would you be open to a case study?") collapse the response rate.

A seventh element — gratitude — sits at the close, but the corpus pattern is clear: it should be specific, not boilerplate. "Thanks for letting me put words in your mouth — I know it's a weird ask" lands. "Thanks so much for your time!" doesn't.

## Cold-ask variant: what changes

Same six elements, three differences:

- **Opening leans on outcome data + brokered context, not a recent moment.** "Your team's reporting time dropped 40% in Q3 — Sarah on our CS team suggested you'd be the right person to speak to that." Names the numbers, names the broker, signals you've done your homework.
- **Slightly more business-context framing.** Why the quote matters (which prospect / which deal / which use case it'll unlock) — because the cold-ask customer needs a reason to spend the relationship capital.
- **The pre-drafted quote does even more work.** In a cold ask, the draft is the proof that you understand their outcome and can speak in their register. A bad draft kills the cold ask. A good draft *is* the ask.

## Channel notes

The template renders two formats per variant: **email** (the canonical default) and **Slack/DM** (shorter, punchier, used when you have a real-time channel). In-person/Zoom asks are scripted as a 30-second talking outline that wraps the same six elements but spoken — included as a third sub-variant inside each of warm and cold.

Email is default because it leaves a paper trail the customer can forward to their legal/comms team, which most B2B testimonials require.

## Timing — when to ask

The corpus pattern is unambiguous: **right after a specific win, in the same conversation or within 24 hours.** When a customer just told you something worked — a metric moved, a hire was made, a deadline was hit because of you — that's the window, and it closes fast. Quarterly check-ins, renewal calls, and EBRs are *worse* moments because the customer is in scrutiny mode, not gratitude mode.

The template encodes this as a pre-work check: "what specific win are you asking against, and how recent is it?" If the answer is "I'm not sure" or "it's been a while," the template pushes back: warm up the relationship first, generate a moment, then ask.

## Persona — who you ask

The corpus is sharp on this: the quote needs to come from a **title that resonates with your buyer**, not from whoever loves you most internally. Champion-level loyalty isn't the bar; persona-level credibility is. If your prospects are VPs of Finance, a quote from a Senior Analyst — even a delighted one — does less work than a tepid quote from a Director of Finance.

This shows up as a pre-work decision: "whose voice does the prospect need to hear?" The answer drives whether you ask the champion directly (their title resonates) or ask the champion to broker an intro to their boss (cold-ask territory).

## Template design decisions

**Authority:** corpus-only, with practitioner unanimity on the six-element anatomy and the warm/cold split. No book frame to defer to here.

**Two variants, not one, not three.** Warm and cold(er). A "lukewarm" middle variant was rejected — it dilutes the craft, and most customer-marketing teams already know to dial intensity inside one of the two named variants.

**Three channel sub-formats per variant: email, Slack/DM, in-person/Zoom.** Email default. Slack shorter. In-person scripted as a spoken outline.

**Pre-work as Step 0.** Four pre-work checks before drafting: which specific win, who to ask (persona-credibility filter), where you'll use it (channel + asset), what title resonates with your prospect.

**Pre-drafted quote as a required field**, not an optional flourish. The template enforces this — the section will not render meaningfully without a draft. If the user cannot draft a quote in the customer's voice grounded in a specific outcome, the ask isn't ready and the template flags it.

**Single-question close** as a structural rule, not a tone preference. The skeleton renders exactly one closing question per ask.

**Composition pointers:**
- Resulting quote feeds: `landing_page_copy`, `sales_pitch_deck`, `battlecard`, `one_pager_solution_brief`
- Long-form sibling: `case_study` (artifact 37) — different ask, different commitment, separate template

## System prompt failure modes (negative guidance)

Distilled to 8:

1. **Generic opening.** "Hope you're well!" / "We're building out our customer story library." The opening is about *them* and a specific moment or outcome, not about you and your roadmap.
2. **No pre-drafted quote.** Asking the customer to write from scratch is asking for unpaid work. Always include a draft for them to react to.
3. **Vague usage.** "For some marketing materials" reads as legal risk. Name the channel and the asset specifically.
4. **Vague time commitment.** "A few minutes of your time" feels like a trap. Name the minutes.
5. **Multi-question close.** Stacking asks ("a quote, a video, a case study, a webinar") collapses response rate. One ask per message.
6. **Wrong-persona ask.** Asking the most enthusiastic customer when the buyer needs to hear from a higher title. Persona credibility > champion loyalty.
7. **Wrong-moment ask.** Quarterly check-ins, renewal calls, and EBRs are scrutiny moments, not gratitude moments. Ask right after a specific win.
8. **Too long.** If the email reads in more than 45 seconds, you've lost them. Cut everything that doesn't serve the ask.

Voice rule: warm, specific, brief, low-pressure. No author name-drops in the output.
