## What the books establish

### Dunford 8-step (the spine — REFRAMED for partners)

Dunford's 8-step is built for a *customer* deal. It does not work verbatim for
a partner pitch — the audience, the question being answered, and the Ask are
different. The mapping below is the load-bearing decision of this template.

| Customer pitch (Dunford) | Partner pitch (this artifact) | What changed |
|---|---|---|
| Insight (market shift in buyer's world) | Insight (shift in **the partner's customers'** world) | Frame is the partner's book of business, not your TAM |
| Alternatives (what buyer does today) | Alternatives (what the partner currently offers / can't offer) | Reveals the gap in the partner's portfolio, not the customer's stack |
| Perfect World (criteria for ideal solution) | Perfect Partnership (criteria for what a great partner program looks like in this category) | Criteria the partner uses to evaluate vendors, not the customer's purchase criteria |
| Introduction (your product) | Introduction (your product + program) | Two intros, not one — product is necessary but not sufficient |
| Differentiated Value (3-5 reasons to choose you) | Partner Value Prop, three lanes: economics, lead-gen, differentiation | Hard-locked to three lanes per corpus consensus |
| Proof (customer story) | Joint Customer Story (partnership narrative) | Partner co-stars; not "customer X loves us" but "partner Y co-sold a deal" |
| Objections | Objections (channel conflict + deal protection lead) | Channel conflict is the universal unspoken objection |
| Ask (next step) | Ask + 90-day success criteria | Specificity signals you've run a partner program before |

The **two-phase** structure (Setup / Follow-Through) holds: Setup earns the
right to introduce the product *and the program*; Follow-Through proves both
and closes. The partner is in the room because their economics are squeezed,
their customers are asking new questions, or their existing portfolio is
losing differentiation — Setup must land on at least one of those.

### Duarte craft layer

- **Audience-as-hero / mentor stance.** The partner exec is the hero of this
  story. You are Yoda — the vendor offering tools, training, and pipeline so
  the partner can serve their customers better. Decks that read as "look how
  great we are" miss this completely. The partner's *customer* is the
  ultimate hero; the partner is the agent who serves them; you are the mentor
  to the partner.
- **Sparkline oscillation.** What Is / What Could Be — applied to the
  partner's business. "Today you can't answer when your enterprise customers
  ask about [problem]. With us, you can — and here's the margin." Slides that
  stay in "What Could Be" without grounding in "What Is" feel like vendor
  vapor.
- **One S.T.A.R. moment.** One named partner who closed one named deal in one
  named timeframe with one specific dollar figure. That's what the partner exec
  repeats to their RevOps lead on the call afterward.

Hero's Journey is rejected here for the same reasons as artifact 03 / 14 —
fuzzy in B2B, hard to land objection-handling on top of.

### What the corpus added beyond the books

- **The 3-lane partner value prop** (economics / lead-gen / differentiation).
  Neither Dunford nor Duarte names this; corpus consensus is unambiguous and
  this becomes a skeleton section, not a free-form one.
- **Channel conflict / deal protection as a *load-bearing* slide**, not a
  buried objection. Partners have been burned. Address directly: registration,
  conflict resolution, renewal margin protection.
- **Joint Customer Profile section.** Most decks skip. Without it the partner
  cannot self-qualify and you get "sounds interesting, let's stay in touch"
  with zero pipeline.
- **90-day success criteria in the Ask.** Specificity signals you have run a
  partner program before — one co-sold deal, three certified reps, joint
  pipeline review. Vague asks read as "we have not done this before."
- **Vague enablement = #2 failure mode.** "Robust partner portal" / "training
  and support" mean nothing. Enablement must show scaffolding: certification
  path, time-to-first-deal, who's on the co-sell call.

### Where they disagreed — and which won

| Disagreement | Position A | Position B | Winner | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 8-step verbatim vs. partner-specific spine | Dunford 8-step holds | Corpus 7-section partner-specific structure | **Hybrid** | Dunford's spine *re-mapped* (the table above). Verbatim Dunford produces a customer pitch; verbatim corpus loses the Setup discipline (Insight → Alternatives → Perfect World) that earns the right to introduce the program. |
| Three lanes vs. free-form value prop | Corpus locks 3 lanes | Some practitioners use 5+ value pillars | **Corpus 3 lanes** | Same reasoning as Punchy's 3-benefit rule (artifact 02): four becomes inventory; partners reading a 6-pillar slide assume you can't pick. Economics + lead-gen + differentiation is the canonical partner-decision triangle. |
| Channel conflict — objection slide vs. dedicated section | Dunford treats as Step 7 (objections) | Corpus treats as a load-bearing section | **Corpus** | Channel conflict is *the* unspoken question in every partner conversation. Burying it in objections signals evasion. Promote it. |
| Partner program slide(s) vs. partner program implicit | Implicit in "Differentiated Value" | Explicit Enablement + Deal Protection slides | **Corpus** | The partner exec is choosing a *program*, not a product. Implicit handling lets the deck collapse back into a customer pitch. |

### Where they merged

- **Setup phase (Insight → Alternatives → Perfect Partnership).** Dunford's
  Setup discipline applied with partner framing. Skipping the Setup is the
  same failure mode as a customer pitch — the partner thinks "why are you
  pitching me this when I already have three vendors in this category?" The
  Insight earns the right.
- **One S.T.A.R. moment + customer story slide.** Duarte's S.T.A.R. lands on
  the Joint Customer Story — one named partner, one named deal, one specific
  outcome. The line the partner exec repeats internally.
- **Sparkline + 8-step ordering.** The 8-step IS a Sparkline if you read the
  ordering: Insight (What Is, deepening) → Alternatives (What Is, painful) →
  Perfect Partnership (What Could Be, criteria) → Introduction (What Could Be,
  named) → Value Prop (What Could Be, quantified) → Joint Customer Story
  (What Could Be, real) → Objections (What Is, addressed) → Ask (cross the
  threshold). This is the unifying frame.

## Template design decisions

**Authority hierarchy:** Dunford 8-step drives the spine (re-mapped to partner
audience). Duarte gives the rhythm (Sparkline) and stance (mentor, audience-as-
hero). Corpus locks the partner-specific sections that neither book names —
3-lane value prop, joint customer profile, dedicated enablement + deal
protection, 90-day success criteria.

**11 slides + pre-work + quality gate + validation.** The slide count is
informed by the corpus ceiling (~12-15 for a virtual partner pitch, similar
range to artifact 14). Specifically:

- Slide 1 — Opening hook / Insight (the partner's customers' world is shifting)
- Slides 2-3 — Alternatives + Perfect Partnership (what the partner currently
  has / can't offer; what an ideal partnership in this category looks like)
- Slide 4 — Introduction (the product + the program — two intros, not one)
- Slides 5-7 — Partner Value Proposition (three lanes, one slide each:
  Economics, Lead-Gen, Differentiation)
- Slide 8 — Enablement (certification, time-to-first-deal, co-sell motion)
- Slide 9 — Deal Protection (channel conflict, registration, renewal margin)
- Slide 10 — Joint Customer Story (one named partner + named deal — S.T.A.R.)
- Slide 11 — Ask + 90-day success criteria

**Three-lane value prop is structurally enforced.** Three slides, one per
lane, each with bracketed prompts that resist drift. The model cannot collapse
"economics" into a sentence on a generic value slide.

**Channel conflict / deal protection is a dedicated slide, not buried in
objections.** This is the corpus's sharpest contribution.

**Joint Customer Profile lands inside the Perfect Partnership slide** rather
than as a standalone slide. The corpus surfaced JCP as a separate section,
but in deck form it reads more naturally as "here's the customer we win
together" embedded in the criteria for an ideal partnership. The pre-work
locks JCP explicitly so it cannot be skipped.

**Pre-work block** mirrors artifacts 01, 03, 14. Locks: partner motion type
(resell / co-sell / refer / OEM — different programs, different decks),
joint customer profile (industry, size, trigger, ICP overlap), the partner's
current portfolio gap (the Insight target), the planted S.T.A.R. moment, and
the inherited upstream artifacts.

**Mentor stance, not hero.** The partner is the hero of their customers'
story; you are the mentor to the partner. Body slides live in the partner's
reality (their deals, their pipeline, their customer conversations), not yours.

**Failure modes in system prompt:**
1. Customer pitch repurposed for partners ("our customers love us" on slide 2)
2. No economics / vague margin structure (silence reads as evasion)
3. Vague enablement ("robust partner portal" — partners have been promised
   portals)
4. No joint customer profile (partner can't self-qualify)
5. Channel conflict buried in objections instead of surfaced
6. Vendor name-drops as the enemy (collapses to a battlecard)
7. Hero's Journey drift
8. More than one CTA in the Ask
9. Lead with vision / company mission instead of partner's customers' shift
10. Inside-out language ("we built", "our team is excited")

## Sections excluded

- **Logo wall / "thanks for joining"** — wastes the first 30 seconds (artifact
  14 failure mode applies here too).
- **Founder story / company mission** — partner doesn't care; their customers
  care about their own outcomes.
- **Full product walkthrough / feature inventory** — the deck is about the
  *partnership*, not a product demo. Demo lives in a follow-up enablement
  session, not the recruitment pitch.
- **Co-sell battlecard** — that's artifact 28, used after partnership exists.
- **Joint solution brief** — that's artifact 29, also post-partnership.
- **Detailed partner enablement curriculum** — that's artifact 27. This deck
  surfaces the *shape* of enablement, not the curriculum.
- **Tiering / partner program tiers (Silver/Gold/Platinum)** — adjacent
  artifact (partner program design); name the tier this partner is being
  recruited into via pre-work, do not enumerate all tiers in the recruitment
  deck.
- **Legal terms / contract details** — separate artifact (partner agreement).

## How this artifact differs from adjacent artifacts

**vs. artifact 19 (sales_pitch_deck):** Same Dunford spine, different
audience, different Ask. A sales pitch deck recruits a *customer*; this deck
recruits a *partner*. Customer pitch answers "will this solve my problem?";
partner pitch answers "will this help me make money and look good to my
customers?" Different question → different value prop frame (3 lanes:
economics / lead-gen / differentiation, not customer outcome themes) →
different proof (joint customer story with partner co-starring, not "customer
X loves us") → different Ask (program enrollment + 90-day criteria, not
commercial close).

**vs. artifact 28 (co_sell_battlecard):** Co-sell battlecard is a tactical
asset used by an *existing* partner during a live deal — competitor by
competitor, what to say, what to lead with. This deck *recruits* the partner;
it precedes the battlecard temporally and structurally.

**vs. artifact 29 (joint_solution_brief):** Joint solution brief is an
external customer-facing one-pager that names the partner + the vendor + the
combined offering. Used after a partnership exists, sold *to customers*. This
deck is internal-to-the-partnership, sold *to the partner*, before the
partnership exists.

**vs. artifact 27 (partner_enablement_one_pager):** Enablement one-pager is
a post-signing asset — once the partner has signed, this is the page that
trains their reps. Slide 8 of this deck shows the *shape* of enablement; the
one-pager is the actual training artifact.

## Open questions for audit

- **Partner motion type as pre-work, not as a deck variant.** Different
  partner types (resell / co-sell / refer / OEM) have different economics
  shapes — should the template render four variants? Current decision: one
  template, pre-work locks motion type, the model adapts the Economics slide
  prompt accordingly. Argument for variants: each motion has materially
  different deal-protection mechanics. Argument against (chosen): four
  variants is template sprawl; the pre-work is sufficient if the model
  follows it.
- **Joint Customer Profile — embedded in Perfect Partnership slide vs.
  standalone slide.** Currently embedded. Argument for standalone: corpus
  treats it as a section. Argument against (chosen): in a deck (vs. a
  document), it reads more naturally inside Perfect Partnership and the
  pre-work locks JCP explicitly so it cannot be skipped.
- **Live program economics (margin %) vs. directional ("attractive
  margin").** The template prompts for specifics ("specifics, not ranges")
  per corpus consensus, but some companies will not share final economics
  in a recruitment pitch. Open: does the template need a "directional vs.
  signed NDA" branch, or does the prompt's specificity language do enough?
  Current decision: prompt for specifics; if user pushes back, that's a
  signal their economics aren't ready for partner pitch (which is its own
  failure mode).
- **Corpus gaps:** No PartnerStack / Crossbeam / Allbound branded content
  surfaced as a *named* citation despite the practitioner pattern matching
  their playbooks. Likely an ingestion gap for partner-ecosystem thought
  leadership specifically. Logged in `corpus-gaps.md` for the next sweep.
