## What the books establish

### Sales Pitch (Dunford) — the structural backbone

Dunford explicitly **rejects** four common pitch approaches:
1. Product walkthrough (features-first)
2. Problem/solution (skips why alternatives fail)
3. Vision narrative (too abstract, doesn't survive first objection)
4. Hero's Journey (structurally fuzzy, hard to adapt across stakeholders)

The alternative — and this is the load-bearing claim — is a **structured insight-led pitch built around positioning**. Two phases:
- **Setup (Steps 1–3): Insight → Alternatives → Perfect World.** Earns the right to introduce the product. Starts with a market shift, makes existing alternatives feel inadequate, defines what an ideal solution would look like (engineered to match your strengths but framed objectively).
- **Follow-Through (Steps 4–8): Introduction → Differentiated Value → Proof → Objections → Ask.** Reveal the product, prove the value, close.

Key principle from the card: *"The pitch earns the right to introduce the product. The Setup creates the context where the introduction is inevitable."* And: *"You cannot pitch your way out of a positioning problem."*

### Stories that Stick (Hall) — the craft layer

Hall's contribution is narrative craft, not structure:
- **Four story elements** every story needs: Identifiable Characters, Authentic Emotion, Significant Moment, Specific Details.
- **Normal → Explosion → New Normal** — the universal arc. Strategic narratives are explosion-pivot stories at the market level: *"the market was operating in normal — then something blew up — and the new normal looks like this."*
- **Four business stories taxonomy:** Value, Founder, Purpose, Customer. Strategic narrative is *most aligned with the Value Story slot* (why the offering matters, applied to the market level rather than the product level).
- The Gap Theory: stories work when they expose a gap between expectation and reality. The strategic narrative's "shift" is exactly that gap, named at the industry level.

## What the corpus added (Tier 2 amplification)

The corpus query surfaced a coherent practitioner model — call it the **5-component strategic narrative**:

1. **The shift** — a specific, named, undeniable change in the buyer's world. HubSpot's: "buyers stopped answering cold calls and started Googling." Not "the market is evolving."
2. **The stakes** — what happens to companies that ignore the shift. Tragedy framing creates urgency without manufactured pressure.
3. **The promised land** — a vision of the buyer's transformed world if they win the new game. NOT a description of your product.
4. **The obstacles** — what stands between the buyer and the promised land. Where your product's problem-set surfaces, framed as the buyer's journey.
5. **Your role as guide** — Yoda, not Luke. The buyer is the hero.

The corpus also surfaced an explicit, useful **distinction** that the books don't draw cleanly:
- **Strategic narrative is the movement** — unites buyers, employees, investors, press; survives product pivots.
- **Sales pitch is the buyer-specific application** — present-tense, "why us over alternatives." Borrows the narrative's framing for setup.
- **Category creation is a possible outcome, not a strategy** — Slack-scale category creation requires industry shift + competitors following + years of execution. Most attempts end in no-man's-land.

## Reconciliation: book vs. corpus

The books and corpus do not directly conflict — they describe **different layers of the same artifact**. Reconciliation:

### Where they merge cleanly

The Raskin 5-component model maps onto Dunford's Setup phase + the front of the Follow-Through:

| Raskin component | Dunford 8-step equivalent | Hall element |
|---|---|---|
| The shift | Step 1: Insight | "Explosion" — the inciting market event |
| The stakes | (Implicit in Step 1; corpus adds explicit stakes) | Authentic emotion (urgency, fear-of-loss) |
| The promised land | Step 3: Perfect World (re-framed as buyer outcome, not solution criteria) | "New Normal" |
| The obstacles | Step 2: Alternatives + Step 5: Differentiated Value | Significant moment (turning point) |
| Your role as guide | Step 4: Introduction (re-framed as guide, not hero) | Identifiable characters (buyer = hero) |

The merge: **use Dunford's structural ordering, but adopt Raskin's stakes-and-promised-land vocabulary** because the corpus is right that Dunford's "Perfect World" gets confused with "describe your product in disguise." Naming the stakes explicitly and naming the promised land as a *world the buyer lives in* is sharper.

### Where canonical wins

- **Strategic narrative is NOT the Hero's Journey.** Dunford explicitly rejects Hero's Journey for B2B because it's structurally fuzzy. Some Raskin disciples conflate "you're the guide" with "the buyer is on a hero's journey." We follow Dunford: the narrative is *insight-led*, not journey-led. The buyer has a shift to navigate, not a quest to complete.
- **Don't lead with vision.** Dunford rejects pitch-by-vision. The strategic narrative starts with the shift (a thing that already happened in the buyer's world), not a future vision. Vision shows up in the promised land — *after* stakes are established.

### Where corpus wins

- **Strategic narrative ≠ sales pitch.** Dunford's book is about the sales pitch and largely treats the narrative-deck and the sales-pitch-deck as the same artifact. The corpus is sharper here: **the strategic narrative is the company-level story; the sales pitch is its buyer-specific application.** This boundary is essential for our 39-template universe (artifact 03 vs. artifact 19).
- **Name the enemy without naming a competitor.** The corpus surfaces the "named enemy" pattern (Raskin's vocabulary) — but the enemy is the *old way of doing things*, not a vendor. Calling out a vendor by name turns the narrative into a competitive battlecard.

## Strategic narrative vs. sales pitch deck (the boundary that matters)

This template (artifact 03) and the upcoming sales pitch deck (artifact 19) share DNA. The boundary:

| | Strategic Narrative (artifact 03) | Sales Pitch Deck (artifact 19) |
|---|---|---|
| **Audience** | All audiences: prospects, customers, employees, investors, analysts, press | The champion in a specific deal |
| **Time horizon** | The future the company is betting on | The next 30–90 days of this deal |
| **Specificity** | Industry-level, role-agnostic at the top of the funnel | Buyer-specific, customized per ICP and stage |
| **Output format** | A written narrative document (not a deck) — used as the source for keynotes, decks, blogs, all-hands | A 10–15-slide sales deck delivered live by an AE |
| **Closes with** | "This is the world we're building toward, and why" | A specific Ask: demo, intro, decision |
| **Cadence** | Updated annually (or when positioning shifts) | Iterated continuously per win/loss feedback |
| **Owner** | CEO + PMM + Founder | PMM + Sales enablement + AE |

The strategic narrative *feeds* the sales pitch deck — Steps 1–3 of the sales pitch (Insight, Alternatives, Perfect World) should derive directly from the strategic narrative's shift, stakes, and promised land. If they don't align, the company is telling two stories.

### Strategic narrative vs. positioning statement (artifact 01) — the other boundary

- **Positioning statement** is the internal strategic doc: 5 components (Competitive Alternatives, Distinct Capabilities, Differentiated Value, Best-Fit Accounts, Market Category). Not a story — a structured set of decisions.
- **Strategic narrative** is the externally-facing story that *expresses* those decisions. The narrative can't be written before positioning; the positioning is mute without a narrative.

If positioning is the architecture, the strategic narrative is the building's story — what it's for, why it's there, who it serves.

## Template design decisions

**Authority hierarchy:** Dunford's two-phase structure is the spine. Hall's craft elements (characters, emotion, moment, details) are quality gates. Raskin's 5-component vocabulary is overlaid on the spine because it's sharper for the company-level frame.

**Hybrid framework, explicitly named in the skeleton.** Six sections: Shift → Stakes → Old Game vs. New Game → Promised Land → Obstacles → Your Role as Guide. This compresses Dunford's 8-step (which is built for a buyer pitch) into a company-level narrative. We deliberately drop Steps 6–8 (Proof, Objections, Ask) because those are sales-pitch concerns, not narrative concerns — they belong to artifact 19.

**Old game / new game as a section, not a buried claim.** The corpus repeatedly surfaces "the old way of winning no longer works, and there's a new game." Making this explicit forces clarity: what *exactly* changed, and what's the new winning move?

**Stakes BEFORE promised land.** Loss aversion outperforms gain framing in B2B. The stakes section earns the right to the promised land. Reversing this order produces aspirational fluff.

**Promised land in the buyer's world, not the product's.** The most common failure mode (per corpus). Skeleton bracketed prompt enforces this: "describe the buyer's transformed world, not your platform's screens."

**Pre-work block** (mirrors positioning template). Two decisions: (1) is this for a movement or a launch? — affects narrative breadth; (2) is the shift confirmable or does it need education? — Considered vs. Unconsidered framing from Dunford. Education-mode narratives need stronger Insight + more Proof later.

**No "named enemy as competitor" section.** The corpus pattern is to name the *old way* as the enemy ("manual outbound," "siloed dashboards"), not a vendor. The skeleton's Old Game section captures this; we don't need a separate competitor-call-out.

## Section-by-section rationale

| Section | Source | Why included |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-work | Dunford (Considered/Unconsidered, multi-product) | Forces the "what kind of narrative is this" decision before drafting |
| The Shift | Dunford Step 1 + Raskin "shift" | The opening move. The buyer has to nod within 30s |
| The Stakes | Raskin (corpus) | Loss-aversion urgency. Books understate this |
| Old Game vs. New Game | Raskin (corpus) + Dunford Step 2 | Forces concrete naming of what stopped working |
| The Promised Land | Raskin (corpus) + Dunford Step 3 (re-framed) | Buyer's transformed world. The vision payoff |
| The Obstacles | Dunford Step 2 (Alternatives) + Step 5 (Differentiated Value abstracted) | What's between the buyer and the promised land |
| Your Role as Guide | Dunford Step 4 (re-framed) | Company's role — Yoda, not Luke |
| Narrative quality gate | Hall (four elements) | Self-check: characters / emotion / moment / details |
| Validation: anchor sentence | Corpus | "The world has changed because..." finishes with something the buyer already believes |

## Sections excluded

- **Proof / case studies** — belong to messaging framework + case study + sales pitch. Strategic narrative is pre-proof; it's the frame proof gets slotted into.
- **Objection handling** — sales pitch concern (artifact 19), not narrative.
- **The Ask / CTA** — sales pitch concern. The narrative doesn't close a deal; it earns the right to have a conversation.
- **Differentiated value detail / capability lists** — that's positioning (artifact 01) and messaging framework (artifact 02). The narrative *uses* differentiation, doesn't enumerate it.
- **Hero's Journey arc** — Dunford rejects it for B2B; we follow.
- **Founder / Purpose stories** — Hall's other three story types. Founder and Purpose stories are adjacent artifacts (founder bio, mission statement) — not the strategic narrative.

## System prompt failure modes (negative guidance)

Distilled to 6:

1. **Vague shift** — "the market is evolving," "AI is changing everything." A weather report, not a shift. Forces the model to name a specific, concrete, undeniable change.
2. **Promised land described as the product** — "a world where you use our platform." The promised land lives in the buyer's reality, not yours.
3. **Stakes too soft** — "you'll miss out on growth." Reframe as concrete tragedy: what dries up, what gets eaten, what stops working.
4. **Hero's Journey drift** — making the buyer a "hero on a quest." Dunford rejected this for B2B. The buyer has a shift to navigate, not a quest to complete.
5. **Vision without evidence** — pure aspiration with no proof the shift is real. The shift must be confirmable from the buyer's own experience or external data.
6. **Named-enemy-as-vendor** — calling out a competitor by name. The enemy is the *old way*, not a vendor. Vendor call-outs belong on a battlecard.

Plus one **positive ask**: pull from PMM Sherpa's RAG corpus to ground the shift in real industry evidence (analyst reports, customer behavior data, named market movements) — not invented trends.
