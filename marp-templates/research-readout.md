# Research Readout: Why Companies Evaluate Revenue Intelligence
## Category Evaluation Study — Spring 2026

<br>

**Participants:** 15 discovery interviews
**Date range:** March 3 – April 18, 2026
**Researcher:** Priya Mehta, Senior PMM — Customer Insights

<br>

`PRIMARY RESEARCH` `15 PARTICIPANTS` `B2B SaaS` `REVENUE INTELLIGENCE`

---

## Research Goals & Method

**Three research questions guiding this study:**

1. What triggers a company to evaluate a new revenue intelligence (RI) platform — and what does "good enough" look like before they act?
2. Who drives the evaluation (economic buyer, champion, end user) and how does that shape the criteria?
3. What are the moments of truth that determine which vendor wins — and which loses?

**Methodology:** 45-minute semi-structured discovery interviews via Zoom
**Participant profile:** Revenue leaders (CROs, VPs of Sales) and RevOps practitioners who had evaluated an RI platform in the past 18 months
**Recruitment:** Mix of won customers (n=8), lost deals (n=4), and active prospects who evaluated but did not purchase (n=3). Recruited via CS team intros, LinkedIn outreach, and a Pavilion community post.

## Key Findings Overview

Five headline findings — and why each one matters.

1. **The trigger is almost never planned.** Evaluations start from a breaking-point event, not a scheduled review cycle. *So what:* Messaging that speaks to symptoms (deal slippage, forecast misses) outperforms category-level awareness content.

2. **RevOps owns the evaluation, but the CRO owns the budget.** The champion is almost always in operations; the economic buyer is almost always in sales leadership. These two have different success criteria. *So what:* We need separate value props — workflow efficiency for RevOps, pipeline outcomes for CRO.

3. **Time-to-value is the primary vendor selection criterion, not features.** More than pricing or depth, buyers select the vendor they believe will show ROI fastest. *So what:* Our onboarding narrative is a competitive differentiator we're not using.

4. **The "free trial" moment is where deals are lost.** Three of four lost deals cited the trial experience as the decision point — not the pitch or pricing. *So what:* We need a structured success milestone in week 1 of trial, not a blank canvas.

5. **Gong's brand halo costs us 2–3 weeks per deal cycle.** Buyers enter evaluations with Gong as the default assumption. Dethroning requires a structured first-meeting reframe. *So what:* The opening question we ask shapes the entire eval.

## Finding 1 (Deep Dive): Evaluations Start from a Breaking Point

**The most important finding: Category purchase decisions are reactive, not proactive.**

**Evidence:** 11 of 15 participants described a specific, memorable event that triggered the evaluation — a missed forecast, a rep leaving with no captured knowledge, a board question they couldn't answer with data. Only 4 started from a scheduled vendor review or budget process.

*"We had a Q3 where three of our top AEs missed quota and I couldn't tell you why from any system I had. That was the moment. I didn't care what it cost — I needed to be able to see what was happening in my pipeline."*
— CRO, 350-person HR Tech company (Purchased)

*"RevOps came to me after our worst forecast miss in two years with a spreadsheet trying to explain where deals went. That's when I said we need to fix this. I didn't even know 'revenue intelligence' was a category until we started looking."*
— VP Sales, 180-person FinTech (Purchased)

**Implications:**
- Top-of-funnel content should speak to the breaking-point symptoms, not the category (e.g., "Why your forecast keeps missing" beats "What is revenue intelligence?")
- Sales plays should include a "breaking point" discovery question as the first line of inquiry
- Retargeting campaigns should be triggered by intent signals around forecast accuracy and deal slippage — not RI category terms

---

## Finding 2 (Deep Dive): RevOps Champions, CROs Buy

**Second finding: A dual-buyer dynamic creates misaligned evaluation criteria within the same deal.**

**Evidence:** In 12 of 15 evaluations, the champion running the evaluation was a RevOps Manager or Head of Enablement. In 10 of those 12 cases, the economic buyer was the CRO or VP of Sales — someone one or two levels above the champion who was not in the majority of evaluation calls.

*"I built the whole scorecard, ran the vendor demos, and made the recommendation. But when it went to the CRO, the first question was 'what's the pipeline impact?' — not 'how does the workflow work?' I had to rebuild my whole deck."*
— RevOps Manager, 420-person MarTech company (Purchased)

*"My team evaluated three vendors over six weeks. When I finally reviewed the output, I didn't care about feature matrices. I asked: which one will make my team close more deals by Q3? That's a different question than what they had answered."*
— CRO, 280-person DevTools company (Chose competitor)

**Implications:**
- Build two parallel decks: a workflow/ROI deck for RevOps champions, and a pipeline-outcome executive summary for CRO sign-off
- Do not let champions go into exec reviews without a "CRO translation" document from our AE
- Our current demo is optimized for RevOps; we need a 10-minute exec version that leads with pipeline math

---

## Finding 3 (Deep Dive): Time-to-Value Beats Features

**Third finding: The vendor who can prove ROI fastest wins — not the vendor with the deepest feature set.**

**Evidence:** When asked to rank evaluation criteria unprompted, "time to first value" appeared in the top 3 for 13 of 15 participants. "Feature depth" appeared in the top 3 for only 5. Pricing appeared in the top 3 for 7 — but in 6 of those cases, it was invoked as a tiebreaker, not a primary driver.

*"We asked every vendor: when will we see our first useful insight? Gong said 30 days. You said 2 weeks. That was the difference — we had a board meeting in 3 weeks and I needed something to show."*
— VP Sales, 510-person Logistics SaaS (Purchased)

*"The vendor that won had a 'day 7 success' framework — they told us exactly what we'd have at the end of week 1. Everyone else gave us a deployment timeline. That's not the same thing."*
— RevOps Manager, 175-person PLG company (Purchased)

**Implications:**
- Create a "Day 7 Promise" framework that defines a concrete, measurable output in the first week of trial or onboarding
- Train AEs to lead with time-to-value positioning in discovery, not feature comparison
- Competitive positioning should include side-by-side "time to first insight" comparison against top competitors

## Recommendations

**Five specific actions tied directly to findings**

| Recommendation | Finding | Owner | Priority |
|----------------|---------|-------|----------|
| Rewrite top-of-funnel content around breaking-point symptoms (forecast misses, rep attrition, deal slippage) — not category education | F1: Reactive triggers | PMM + Content | P0 — Q3 start |
| Build a 10-minute CRO executive summary deck ("pipeline math in 10 slides") to complement the existing RevOps demo and proposal | F2: Dual buyer | PMM + Sales Enablement | P0 — Q3 start |
| Define and launch a "Day 7 Success" framework: one concrete output every new customer achieves in their first 7 days | F3: Time-to-value | PMM + CS | P1 — Q3 mid |
| Redesign trial experience with a structured first-week milestone and guided activation sequence — not a blank product sandbox | F4: Trial is the decision point | PMM + Product | P1 — Q3 mid |
| Develop a "reframe" first-meeting script that acknowledges Gong's brand and pivots to evaluation criteria where we win | F5: Gong halo | PMM + Sales | P2 — Q3 end |

## Limitations & Next Steps

**Research limitations to keep in mind:**

- **Sample size:** 15 interviews is sufficient for qualitative theme saturation but cannot support statistical claims. Quotes and patterns are directional, not definitive.
- **Recruitment bias:** 8 of 15 participants were existing customers (introduced via CS). Won customers may overweight factors that justified their purchase. Lost deal participants may rationalize away true decision drivers.
- **Recall bias:** Evaluations occurred 2–18 months ago. Participant memory of exact evaluation criteria may be reconstructed post-hoc rather than accurately recalled.
- **Single researcher:** All interviews conducted by one researcher. No independent coding of transcripts — themes reflect one analytical lens.

**Next research questions to explore:**

- What does the "trial to close" drop-off look like at the data level? (Quantitative follow-up — CS data + product analytics)
- Does the dual-buyer dynamic differ in PLG vs. sales-led motions? (Requires 8–10 additional PLG-specific interviews)
- How do buyers describe "time to value" in their own language — and does it match how we define it? (Concept testing study, n=20)
