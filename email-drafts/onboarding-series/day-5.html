Stop guessing on pricing

Hi,

Most pricing decisions happen like this: someone opens a spreadsheet, lists competitor prices, adds or subtracts 10%, and ships it. Six months later you realize you're either leaving money on the table or losing deals to a cheaper product with worse features. That's not pricing strategy. That's averaging.

PMM Sherpa draws from Ramanujam's Monetizing Innovation (willingness-to-pay methodology), Kyle Poyar's 140+ posts on SaaS pricing, and Elena Verna's PLG pricing insights. When you need current competitor pricing, it pulls that data in real-time. You get recommendations with reasoning, not a template with blank cells.

Three prompts for pricing strategy

Pressure-test your tiers

"Pressure-test this pricing: [describe your tiers and prices]. Our ICP is [describe them]. Our main competitors charge [what you know]. Where am I leaving money on the table? Where will I lose deals? What would you change?"

Tip: Include your tier names, prices, and what's included in each. Even rough pricing is enough to get useful feedback.

Research competitor pricing

"We're launching a new product in [category] for [audience]. Research current competitor pricing in this space and recommend a pricing model (per-seat, usage-based, tiered flat rate) with specific price points. Justify each recommendation."

Tip: Tell Sherpa your GTM motion (PLG vs. sales-led). A self-serve product needs very different pricing psychology than an enterprise sales product.

Evaluate pricing model switch

"We're considering switching from per-seat to usage-based pricing. Our product is [describe it]. Walk me through the pros and cons using Ramanujam's framework. What metrics should we price on, and what are the risks of getting it wrong?"

Tip: Include your current pricing and rough revenue if you're comfortable. The more context Sherpa has about your business, the more specific it can be about migration risks.
