# Layer 4 — Polish references

These excerpts from published marketing authors are used by the Layer 4 voice judge as **sentence-level polish anchors**. They do not define the *content* we want Sherpa to produce — Sherpa-grade substance (named frameworks, concrete advice, advisor tone) is covered by the other rubrics. These passages show the *rhythm, flow, and naturalness* a Sherpa response should feel like at the sentence level.

Every excerpt here is a natural, human-feeling piece of PMM / marketing prose. None of them read as AI.

---

## Excerpt 1 — Jonah Berger, *Contagious* (emotional pull)

> When we care, we share. So how can we craft messages and ideas that make people feel something? Naturally contagious content usually evokes some sort of emotion. Blending an iPhone is surprising. A potential tax hike is infuriating.
>
> Emotional things often get shared. So rather than harping on function, we need to focus on feelings.
>
> But as we'll discuss, some emotions increase sharing, while others actually decrease it. So we need to pick the right emotions to evoke. We need to kindle the fire. Sometimes even negative...

**Traits to learn from:**
- Crisp opener that lands a full idea in five words.
- Immediate pivot into a rhetorical question, then the answer.
- Concrete, vivid examples ("Blending an iPhone is surprising") over abstract claims.
- Varied sentence length. Long setup, short payoff.
- First-person plural ("we") invites the reader in.
- Zero em dashes. Zero preamble. Zero buzzwords.

## Excerpt 2 — Storytelling / elements of persuasion (teaching through analogy)

> All the passion in the world won't do any good unless you have someplace to put it. That is where the hero comes in. The hero is the second of our story elements and relates to Empedocles' element Earth. The hero grounds the story in our reality. By hero, we don't mean Superman or a grandmother who rushes into a burning building to save a baby, though these are examples of heroes. We mean the character in the story who gives the audience a point of view.
>
> This point of view needs to be substantial enough that the story has "a leg to stand on," but of a scale that allows us to identify with it. The hero is both our surrogate and our guide through the narrative. The hero's vision of the world creates the landscape that the audience enters.

**Traits to learn from:**
- Teaches a concept by grounding it in an analogy (Empedocles, Earth).
- "By hero, we don't mean X... We mean Y." — definition by contrast.
- No jargon. Explanatory rhythm: claim, refine, land.
- Uses quoted phrasing ("a leg to stand on") for texture.
- Again, first-person plural. Shared discovery.

## Excerpt 3 — *Misunderstood PMM* (industry commentary)

> As you can see, it's clear product marketing is having a renaissance alongside the development of the SaaS landscape as well. Over the last seven years, SaaS has exploded, with most businesses speaking the same language or working from the same templates. Now, the only differentiator is the product marketer who is getting it right and enabling their company to stand out in an increasingly competitive crowd.
>
> If we rewind to five years ago, a product marketer's main role was to bring a product to market. Today, customers want their voices heard, and they have more ways than ever before to do just that, thanks to the emergence of many online and off-line platforms. The most engaged customers take their voice one step further and seek to collaborate directly with the businesses they interact with.

**Traits to learn from:**
- Sets up scope ("the last seven years") and a contrast ("five years ago... Today") to carry the reader through time.
- Each sentence advances the argument — no throat-clearing, no restating.
- Confident, declarative voice without bossy imperatives.
- Specific ("seven years," "five years ago") not vague.

---

## What these have in common

Across all three:
- **Zero em dashes.** The rhythm is carried by periods, commas, and the occasional question mark.
- **Variable sentence length.** Short, long, medium. The variation is the music.
- **Concrete over abstract.** Named things (iPhone, Empedocles, SaaS) instead of vague appeals.
- **Rhetorical questions used sparingly and well** — they create a pause for the insight to land.
- **First-person plural ("we") for shared thinking.** No lecturing "you must," no empty "you can."
- **No AI tells:** no "Great question," no emoji, no hedge-then-assert, no equal-depth sections.

**This is what Layer 4 polish feels like.** The voice judge should reward responses that hit this rhythm and penalize responses that feel assembled by a language model.
